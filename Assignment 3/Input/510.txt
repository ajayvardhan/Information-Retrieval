<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mediterranean tropical-like cyclone</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Mediterranean tropical-like cyclones</b>, sometimes referred to as <b>Mediterranean hurricanes</b> or <b>medicanes</b>, are rare meteorological phenomena observed in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. Due to the dry nature of the Mediterranean region, formation of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> is infrequent, with only 99Â recorded tropical-like storms between 1948 and 2011. No agency, however, is officially responsible for monitoring the formation and development of medicanes. <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a> typically occurs within two separate regions of the sea. The first region, encompassing areas of the western Mediterranean, is more conducive for development than the other, the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a> to the east. The rough mountainous geography of the region raises additional difficulties despite being favorable for the development of severe weather and convective activity in general, and only with abnormal meteorological circumstances can medicanes form. Numerous studies have been conducted on the impact of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> on Mediterranean tropical cyclone formation, generally concluding that fewer yet more intense storms would form.</p>
<p>The development of tropical cyclones in the Mediterranean Sea can usually only occur under somewhat unusual circumstances. Low <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> instability induced by incursions of cold air are often required. A majority of medicanes are also accompanied by upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">troughs</a>, providing energy required for intensifying <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">atmospheric convection</a>âthunderstormsâand heavy <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinic</a> properties of the Mediterranean region, with high temperature gradients, also provides necessary instability for the formation of tropical cyclones. Another factor, rising cool air, provides necessary moisture as well. Warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> are mostly unnecessary, however, as most medicanes' energy is derived from warmer air temperatures. When these favorable circumstances coincide, the genesis of warm-core Mediterranean tropical cyclones, often from within existing cut-off <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core lows</a>, is possible in a conducive environment for formation.</p>
<p>Several notable and damaging medicanes are known to have occurred. In September 1969, a north African Mediterranean tropical cyclone produced flooding that killed nearly 600Â individuals, left 250,000Â homeless, and crippled local economies. A medicane in SeptemberÂ 1996 that developed in the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> region spawned six tornadoes and inundated parts of the islands. Several medicanes have also been subject to extensive study, such as those of JanuaryÂ 1982, JanuaryÂ 1995, SeptemberÂ 2006, NovemberÂ 2011, and NovemberÂ 2014. The JanuaryÂ 1995 storm is one of the best-studied Mediterranean tropical cyclones, with its close resemblance to tropical cyclones elsewhere and availability of observations. The medicane of SeptemberÂ 2006, meanwhile, is well-studied due to availability of existing observations and data. In NovemberÂ 2011, the NOAA's <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Analysis_Branch" title="Satellite Analysis Branch">Satellite Analysis Branch</a> monitored a medicane, named Rolf by the <a href="/wiki/Free_University_of_Berlin" title="Free University of Berlin">Free University of Berlin (FU Berlin)</a>, though it ceased doing so the following month. In 2015, the NOAA resumed issuing advisories for tropical systems in the Mediterranean region.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">Climatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Climatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="560" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg/200px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg/300px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg/400px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p><a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> are not considered to be formally classified tropical cyclones and their region of formation is not officially monitored by any agency,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCFAQ_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCFAQ-1">[1]</a></sup> and though the <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Analysis_Branch" title="Satellite Analysis Branch">Satellite Analysis Branch</a> released information related to <a href="#November_2011_.28Rolf.2F01M.29">a medicane in NovemberÂ 2011</a> while it was active, it ceased doing so on 16Â December 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA-bulletins_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-bulletins-2">[2]</a></sup> The Mediterranean Sea, however, lies within the <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> area of responsibility,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AORs_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AORs-3">[3]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>'s <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9t%C3%A9o-France" title="MÃ©tÃ©o-France">MÃ©tÃ©o-France</a> National serves as a "preparation service" for the areas as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AORs3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-AORs3-4">[4]</a></sup> A majority of Mediterranean tropical cyclones form over two separate regions. The first, more conducive for development than the other, encompasses an area of the western Mediterranean bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, southern France, and the shorelines of the islands of <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>. The second identified region of development, in the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a> between <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and Greece and stretching south to <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, is less favorable for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a>. An additional two regions, in the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic</a> seas, produce fewer medicanes, while activity is minimal in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levantine</a> region. The geographical distribution of Mediterranean tropical cyclones is markedly different than that of other <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>, with the formation of regular cyclones centering on the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas</a> mountain ranges, the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Genoa" title="Gulf of Genoa">Gulf of Genoa</a>, and the island of <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-7_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-7-5">[5]</a></sup> Although meteorological factors are most advantageous in the Adriatic and Aegean seas, the closed nature of the region's geography, bordered by land, allows little time for further evolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-18_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-18-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The geography of mountain ranges bordering the Mediterranean are conducive for severe weather and thunderstorms, with the sloped nature of mountainous regions permitting the development of convective activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1470_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1470-7">[7]</a></sup> Although the geography of the Mediterranean region, as well as its dry air, typically prevent the formation of tropical cyclones, when certain meteorological circumstances arise, difficulties influenced by the region's geography are overcome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emanuel2005-220_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel2005-220-8">[8]</a></sup> The occurrence of tropical cyclones in the Mediterranean Sea is generally extremely rare, with an average of 1.57Â forming annually and merely 99Â recorded occurrences of tropical-like storms discovered between 1948 and 2011 in a modern study, with no definitive trend in activity in that period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-6_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-6-9">[9]</a></sup> Few medicanes form during the summer season, though activity typically rises in autumn, peaks in January, and gradually decreases from February to May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-7_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-7-5">[5]</a></sup> In the western Mediterranean region of development, approximately 0.75Â such systems form each year, compared to 0.32Â in the Ionian Sea region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-8_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-8-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Studies have evaluated that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> can result in higher observed intensities of tropical cyclones as a result of deviations in the <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">surface energy flux</a> and atmospheric composition, which both heavily influence the development of medicanes as well. In tropical and subtropical areas, <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> rose 0.2Â Â°C (0.36Â Â°F) within a 50-year period, and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">Western Pacific</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basins</a>, the potential destructiveness and energy of storms nearly doubled within the same duration, evidencing a clear correlation between global warming and tropical cyclone intensities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-9_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-9-11">[11]</a></sup> Within a similarly recent 20-year period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-10_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-10-12">[12]</a></sup> SSTs in the Mediterranean Sea increased by 0.6 to 1Â Â°C (1.1 to 1.8Â Â°F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-9_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-9-11">[11]</a></sup> though no observable increase in medicane activity has been noted, as of yet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-6_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-6-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2006, a computer-driven <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_model" title="Atmospheric model">atmospheric model</a> evaluated the future frequency of Mediterranean cyclones between 2071 and 2100, projecting a decrease in autumn, winter, and spring cyclonic activity coinciding with a dramatic increase in formation near Cyprus, with both scenarios attributed to elevated temperatures as a result of global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anagnostopoulou2006-13_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anagnostopoulou2006-13-13">[13]</a></sup> Other studies, however, have been inconclusive, forecasting both increases and decreases in duration, number, and intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaertner2007-4_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gaertner2007-4-14">[14]</a></sup> Three independent studies, using different methodologies and data, evaluated that while medicane activity would likely decline with a rate depending on the climate scenario considered, a higher percentage of those that formed would be of greater strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2014_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2014-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RomeroEmanuel2013_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-RomeroEmanuel2013-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walsh2014_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh2014-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_characteristics">Development and characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development and characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2476" data-file-width="3260" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg/200px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg/300px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg/400px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Factors required for the formation of medicanes are somewhat different than that normally expected of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>; known to emerge over regions with <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> below 26Â Â°C (79Â Â°F), Mediterranean tropical cyclones often require incursions of colder air to induce atmospheric instability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-7_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-7-5">[5]</a></sup> A majority of medicanes develop above regions of the Mediterranean with SSTs of 15 to 26Â Â°C (59 to 79Â Â°F), with the upper bound only found in the southernmost reaches of the sea. Despite the low sea surface temperatures, the instability incited by cold atmospheric air within a <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinic</a> zoneâregions with high differences in temperature and pressureâpermits the formation of medicanes, in contrast to tropical areas lacking high baroclinity, where raised SSTs are needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-8_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-8-18">[18]</a></sup> While significant deviations in air temperature have been noted around the time of Mediterranean tropical cyclones' formation, few anomalies in sea surface temperature coincide with their development, indicating that the formation of medicanes is primarily controlled by higher air temperatures, not by anomalous SSTs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-14_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-14-19">[19]</a></sup> Similar to tropical cyclones, minimal <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>âdifference in wind speed and direction over a regionâas well as abundant moisture and vorticity encourages the genesis of tropical cyclone-like systems in the Mediterranean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-15_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-15-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the confined character of the Mediterranean and the limited capability of <a href="/wiki/Heat_flux" title="Heat flux">heat fluxes</a>âin the case of medicanes, airâsea heat transferâtropical cyclones with a diameter larger than 300Â km (190Â mi) cannot exist within the Mediterranean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-3_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-3-21">[21]</a></sup> Despite being a relatively baroclinic area with high temperature gradients, the primary energy source utilized by Mediterranean tropical cyclones is derived from underlying heat sources generated by the presence of convectionâthunderstorm activityâin a humid environment, similar to tropical cyclones elsewhere outside the Mediterranean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-5_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-5-22">[22]</a></sup> In comparison to other <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basins</a>, the Mediterranean Sea generally presents a difficult environment for development; although the potential energy necessary for development is not abnormally large, its atmosphere is characterized by its lack of moisture, impeding potential formation. The full development of a medicane often necessitates the formation of a large-scale baroclinic disturbance transitioning late in its life cycle into a tropical cyclone-like system, nearly always under the influence of a deep, cut-off, <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core low</a> within the middle-to-upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, frequently resulting from abnormalities in a wide-spreading <a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">Rossby wave</a>âmassive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meanders" title="Meanders">meanders</a> of upper-atmospheric winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-6_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-6-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_January_28_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The development of medicanes often results from the vertical shift of air in the troposphere as well, resulting in a decrease in its temperature coinciding with an increase in relative humidity, creating an environment more conducive for tropical cyclone formation. This, in turn, leads to in an increase in potential energy, producing heat-induced air-sea instability. Moist air prevents the occurrence of convective <a href="/wiki/Vertical_draft" title="Vertical draft">downdrafts</a>âthe vertically downward movement of airâwhich often hinder the inception of tropical cyclones,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-6_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-6-23">[23]</a></sup> and in such a scenario, wind shear remains minimal; overall, cold-core cut-off lows serve well for the later formation of compact <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">surface flux</a>-influenced warm-core lows such as medicanes. The regular genesis of cold-core upper-level lows and the infrequency of Mediterranean tropical cyclones, however, indicate that additional unusual circumstances are involved the emergence of the latter. Elevated sea surface temperatures, contrasting with cold atmospheric air, encourage atmospheric instability, especially within the troposphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-8_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-8-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In general, most medicanes maintain a radius of 70 to 200Â km (40 to 120Â mi), last between 12Â hours and 5Â days, travel between 700 to 3,000Â km (430 to 1,860Â mi), develop an eye for less than 72Â hours, and feature wind speeds of up to 144Â km/h (89Â mph);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2273_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2273-24">[24]</a></sup> in addition, a majority are characterized on <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a> as asymmetric systems with a distinct round <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> encircled by <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">atmospheric convection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-3_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-3-21">[21]</a></sup> Weak rotation, similar to that in most tropical cyclones, is usually noted in a medicane's early stages, increasing with intensity;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fita2007-43_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fita2007-43-25">[25]</a></sup> medicanes, however, often have less time to intensify, remaining weaker than most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic hurricanes</a> and only persisting for the duration of a few days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fita2007-53_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fita2007-53-26">[26]</a></sup> The maximum achievable intensity of medicanes is equivalent to the lowest classification on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="SaffirâSimpson hurricane wind scale">SaffirâSimpson hurricane wind scale</a>, CategoryÂ 1. While the entire lifetime of a cyclone may encompass several days, most will only retain tropical characteristics for less than 24Â hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miglietta2013-2402_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2013-2402-27">[27]</a></sup> Circumstances sometimes permit the formation of smaller-scale medicanes, although the required conditions differ even from those needed by other medicanes. The development of abnormally small tropical cyclones in the Mediterranean usually requires upper-level atmospheric cyclones inducing cyclogenesis in the lower atmosphere, leading to the formation of warm-core lows, encouraged by favorable moisture, heat, and other environmental circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1469_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1469-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mediterranean cyclones have been compared to <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a>âcyclonic storms which typically develop in the far regions of the Northern and Southern Hemispheresâfor their similarly small size and heat-related instability; however, while medicanes nearly always feature warm-core lows, polar lows are primarily cold-core. The prolonged life of medicanes and similarity to polar lows is caused primarily by origins as synoptic-scale surface lows and heat-related instability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1470_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1470-7">[7]</a></sup> Heavy precipitation and convection within a developing Mediterranean tropical cyclone are usually incited by the approach of an upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>âan elongated area of low air pressuresâbringing downstream cold air, encircling an existing low-pressure system. After this occurs, however, a considerable reduction in rainfall rates occurs despite further organization,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Claud2010-2211_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Claud2010-2211-29">[29]</a></sup> coinciding with a decrease in previously high lightning activity as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miglietta2013-2404_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2013-2404-30">[30]</a></sup> Although troughs will often accompany medicanes along their track, separation eventually occurs, usually in the later part of a Mediterranean tropical cyclone's life cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Claud2010-2211_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Claud2010-2211-29">[29]</a></sup> At the same time, moist air, saturated and cooled while rising into the atmosphere, then encounters the medicane and permits further development and evolution into a tropical cyclone. Many of these characteristics are also evident in polar lows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emanuel2005-217_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel2005-217-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_storms_and_impacts">Notable storms and impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable storms and impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="September_1969">September 1969</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: September 1969">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="241" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png/200px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/db/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png 1.5x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>An unusually severe Mediterranean tropical cyclone developed on 23Â September 1969 southeast of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, producing severe flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup> Steep pressure and temperature gradients above the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas mountain range</a> were evident on 19Â September, a result of cool sea air attempting to penetrate inland; south of the mountains, a lee depressionâa <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> in a mountainous regionâdeveloped. Under the influence of mountainous terrain, the low initially meandered northeastward. Following the entry of cool sea air, however, it recurved to the southeast before transitioning into a Saharan depression associated with a distinct cold front by 22Â September. Along the front's path, desert air moved northward while cold air drifted in the opposite direction, and in northern Libya, warm arid air clashed with the cooler <a href="/wiki/Levant_(wind)" title="Levant (wind)">levant</a> of the Mediterranean. The organization of the disturbance improved slightly further before emerging into the Mediterranean Sea on 23Â September, upon which the system experienced immediate <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">cyclogenesis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-393_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-393-33">[33]</a></sup> rapidly intensifying while southeast of Malta as a cold-core cut-off low,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-396_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-396-34">[34]</a></sup> and acquiring tropical characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup> In western Africa, meanwhile, several disturbances converged toward <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, while the medicane recurved southwestward back toward the coast, losing its closed circulation and later dissipating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-396_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-396-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cyclone produced severe flooding throughout regions of northern Africa. Malta received upward of 123Â mm (4.8Â in) of rainfall on 23Â September, <a href="/wiki/Sfax" title="Sfax">Sfax</a> measured 45Â mm (1.8Â in) on 24Â September, <a href="/wiki/Tizi_Ouzou" title="Tizi Ouzou">Tizi Ouzou</a> collected 55Â mm (2.2Â in) on 25Â September, <a href="/wiki/Gafsa" title="Gafsa">Gafsa</a> received 79Â mm (3.1Â in) and <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> measured 46Â mm (1.8Â in) on 26Â September, Cap Bengut collected 43Â mm (1.7Â in) on 27Â September, and <a href="/wiki/Biskra" title="Biskra">Biskra</a> received 122Â mm (4.8Â in) on 28Â September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-392_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-392-35">[35]</a></sup> In Malta, a 20000-ton tanker struck a reef and split in two, while in Gafsa, Tunisia, the cyclone flooded <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> mines, leaving over 25,000Â miners unemployed and costing the government over Â£2Â million per week. Thousands of camels and snakes, drowned by flood waters, were swept out to sea, and massive <a href="/wiki/Roman_bridge" title="Roman bridge">Roman bridges</a>, which withstood all floods since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decline_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="Decline of the Roman Empire">fall of the Roman Empire</a>, collapsed. In all, the floods in Tunisia and Algeria killed almost 600Â individuals, left 250,000Â homeless, and severely damaged regional economies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-390_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-390-36">[36]</a></sup> Due to communication problems, however, flood relief funds and television appeals were not set up until nearly a month later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-392_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-392-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="January_1982">January 1982</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: January 1982">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The unusual Mediterranean tropical storm of JanuaryÂ 1982 was first detected in waters north of <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup> The storm likely reached the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas mountain range</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> by 23Â January 1982, reinforced by an elongated, slowly-drifting trough above the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>. Eventually, a closed circulation center developed by 1310Â <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-333_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-333-37">[37]</a></sup> over parts of the Mediterranean with <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> of approximately 16Â Â°C (61Â Â°F) and air temperature of 12Â Â°C (54Â Â°F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-334_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-334-38">[38]</a></sup> A hook-shaped cloud developed within the system shortly thereafter, rotating as it elongated into a 150Â km (93Â mi)-long comma-shaped apparatus. After looping around Sicily, it drifted eastward between the island and <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a>, recurving on its track again,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reed2001-187_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reed2001-187-39">[39]</a></sup> exhibiting clearly curved spiral banding before shrinking slightly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reed2001-189_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reed2001-189-40">[40]</a></sup> The cyclone reached its peak intensity at 1800Â UTC on the following day, maintaining an <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> of 992Â <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.30Â <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>), and was succeeded by a period of gradual weakening, with the system's pressure eventually rising to 1009Â mbar (29.80Â inHg). The system slightly reintensified, however, for a six-hour period on 26Â January. Ship reports indicated winds of 93Â km/h (57.7Â mph or 50.2 kt) were present in the cyclone at the time, tropical storm-force winds on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="SaffirâSimpson hurricane wind scale">SaffirâSimpson hurricane wind scale</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-333_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-333-37">[37]</a></sup> likely near the eyewall of the cyclone, which features the highest winds in a tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-334_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-334-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Global Weather Center's Cyclone Weather Center of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force (USAF)</a> initiated "Mediterranean Cyclone Advisories" on the cyclone at six-hour intervals starting at 1800Â UTC on 27Â January, until 0600Â UTC on the following day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-337_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-337-41">[41]</a></sup> Convection was most intense in the eastern sector of the cyclone as it drifted east-northeastward. On infrared satellite imagery, the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> itself was 58.5Â km (36.4Â mi) in diameter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-334_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-334-38">[38]</a></sup> contracting to just 28 kilometres (17Â mi) one day prior to making landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-337_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-337-41">[41]</a></sup> The cyclone passed by Malta, Italy, and Greece before dissipating several days later in the extreme eastern Mediterranean. Observations related to the cyclone, however, were inadequate, and although the system maintained numerous tropical characteristics, it is possible it was merely a compact but powerful <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> exhibiting a clear eye, spiral banding, towering <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbi</a>, and high surface winds along the eyewall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="January_1995">January 1995</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: January 1995">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Among numerous documented medicanes, the cyclone of JanuaryÂ 1995 is generally considered to be the best-documented instance. Emerging off of the <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libyan</a> coast into the central <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> toward the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian</a> shoreline of <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> on 13Â January as a compact <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a>, the precursor low maintained winds reaching up to 108Â km/h (67Â mph) as it traversed the Ionian Sea,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2280_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2280-42">[42]</a></sup> while the German research ship <i>Meteor</i> noted winds of 135Â km/h (84Â mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-263_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-263-43">[43]</a></sup> Upon the low's approach near Greece, it began to envelop an area of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">atmospheric convection</a>; meanwhile, in the middle <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> extended from Russia to the Mediterranean, bringing with it extremely cold temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis1999-628_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis1999-628-44">[44]</a></sup> Two low-pressure areas were present along the path of the trough, with one situated above Ukraine and the other above the central Mediterranean, likely associated with a low-level cyclone over western Greece. Upon weakening and dissipation on 14Â January, a second low, the system which would evolve into the Mediterranean tropical cyclone, developed in its place on 15Â January.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-263_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-263-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of formation, high clouds indicated the presence of intense convection,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-263_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-263-43">[43]</a></sup> and the cyclone featured an axisymmetric cloud structure, with a distinct, cloud-free <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a> spiraling around the disturbance as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-264_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-264-45">[45]</a></sup> Soon thereafter, the parent low separated from the medicane entirely and continued eastward,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis1999-628_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis1999-628-44">[44]</a></sup> meandering toward the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2280_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2280-42">[42]</a></sup> Initially remaining stationary between Greece and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> with a minimum <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> of 1002Â <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.59Â <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>), the newly formed system began to drift southwest-to-south in the following days, influenced by northeasterly flow incited by the initial low, now far to the east, and a <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure area</a> above central and eastern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis1999-628_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis1999-628-44">[44]</a></sup> The system's atmospheric pressure increased throughout 15Â January due to the fact it was embedded within a large-scale environment, with its rising pressure due to the general prevalence of higher air pressures throughout the region, and was not a sign of deintensification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-264_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-264-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initial wind speeds within the young medicane were generally low, with sustained winds of merely 28 to 46Â km/h (17 to 29Â mph), with the highest recorded value associated with the disturbance being 63Â km/h (39Â mph) at 0000Â UTC on 16Â January, slightly below the threshold for tropical storm on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="SaffirâSimpson hurricane wind scale">SaffirâSimpson hurricane wind scale</a>. Its structure now consisted of a distinct eye encircled by counterclockwise-rotating <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbi</a> with <a href="/wiki/Cloud_top" title="Cloud top">cloud top</a> temperatures colder than â50Â Â°C (â58Â Â°F), evidencing deep convection and a regular feature observed in most tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-265_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-265-46">[46]</a></sup> At 1200 UTC on 16 January, a ship recorded winds blowing east-southeast of about 50 knots (93Â km/h) south-southwest about 50Â km (31Â mi) north-northeast of the cyclone's center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlierMa1997_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-BlierMa1997-47">[47]</a></sup> Intense convection continued to follow the entire path of the system as it traversed the Mediterranean, and the cyclone made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in northern Libya at approximately 1800Â <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on 17Â January, rapidly weakening after coming ashore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis1999-628_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis1999-628-44">[44]</a></sup> As it moved inland, a minimum atmospheric pressure of 1012Â mbar (29.89Â inHg) was recorded, accompanied by wind speeds of 93Â km/h (58Â mph) as it slowed down after passing through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Sidra" title="Gulf of Sidra">Gulf of Sidra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-266_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-266-48">[48]</a></sup> Although the system retained its strong convection for several more hours, the cyclone's cloud tops began to warm, evidencing lower clouds, before losing tropical characteristics entirely on 17Â January.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-267_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-267-49">[49]</a></sup> Offshore ship reports recorded that the medicane produced intense winds, copious rainfall, and abnormally warm temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2281_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2281-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="September_and_October_1996">September and October 1996</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: September and October 1996">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Three notable medicanes developed in 1996. The first, in mid-SeptemberÂ 1996, was a typical Mediterranean tropical cyclone that developed in the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282-51">[51]</a></sup> At the time of the cyclone's formation, a powerful Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> associated with a large-scale low, producing northeasterly winds over the Iberian peninsula, extended eastward into the Mediterranean, while abundant moisture gathered in the lower troposphere over the Balearic channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1473_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1473-52">[52]</a></sup> On the morning of 12Â September, a disturbance developed off of <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia, Spain</a>, dropping heavy rainfall on the coast even without coming ashore. An eye developed shortly thereafter as the system rapidly traversed across <a href="/wiki/Majorca" title="Majorca">Majorca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> in its eastward trek. It made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> upon the coast of southern Italy on the evening of 13Â September with a minimum atmospheric pressure of 990Â mbar (29.24Â inHg), dissipating shortly after coming ashore,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2283_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2283-53">[53]</a></sup> with a diameter of about 150Â km (93Â mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1469_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1469-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>At Valencia and other regions of eastern Spain, the storm generated heavy precipitation, while six tornadoes touched down over the Balearic Islands. While approaching the coast of the Balearic Islands, the warm-core low induced a pressure drop of 11Â mbar (0.32Â inHg) at <a href="/wiki/Palma,_Majorca" title="Palma, Majorca">Palma, Majorca</a> in advance of the tropical cyclone's landfall. Medicanes as small as the one that formed in SeptemberÂ 1996 are atypical, and often require circumstances different even from those required for regular Mediterranean tropical cyclone formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1469_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1469-28">[28]</a></sup> Warm low-level <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a>âtransfer of heat through air or seaâcaused by a large-scale low over the western Mediterranean was a primary factor in the rise of strong convection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1470_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1470-7">[7]</a></sup> The presence of a mid- to upper-level cut-off <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core low</a>, a method of formation typical to medicanes, was also key to the development of intense thunderstorms within the cyclone. In addition, interaction between a northeastward-drifting <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>, the medicane, and the large-scale also permitted the formation of tornadoes within thunderstorms generated by the cyclone after making landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1471_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1471-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second of the three recorded Mediterranean tropical cyclones in 1996 formed between Sicily and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> on 4Â October, making landfall on both Sicily and southern Italy. The medicane generated major flooding in Sicily; meanwhile, in <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>, wind gusts of up to 108Â km/h (67Â mph) were reported in addition to severe inundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-4"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup> The third major Mediterranean tropical cyclone of that year formed north of <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and strengthened while sweeping between the Balearic Islands and Sardinia, with an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>-like feature prominent on satellite. The eye was distorted and disappeared after transiting over southern Sardinia throughout the evening of 8Â October, with the system weakening as a whole. On the morning of OctoberÂ 9, a smaller eye emerged as the system passed over the <a href="/wiki/Tyrrhenian_Sea" title="Tyrrhenian Sea">Tyrrhenian Sea</a>, gradually strengthening, with reports 100Â km (62Â mi) from the storm's center reporting winds of 90Â km/h (56Â mph). Extreme damage was reported in the <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_Islands" title="Aeolian Islands">Aeolian Islands</a> after the tropical cyclone passed north of Sicily, though the system dissipated while turning southward over Calabria. Overall, the lowest estimated atmospheric pressure in the third medicane was 998Â mbar (29.47Â inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2284_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2284-55">[55]</a></sup> Both October systems featured distinctive spiral bands, intense convection, high sustained winds, and abundant precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-5"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="September_2006">September 2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: September 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>A short-lived medicane developed near the end of SeptemberÂ 2006 along the coast of Italy. The origins of the medicane can be traced to the alpine <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas mountain range</a> on the evening of 25Â September,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282-51">[51]</a></sup> likely forming as a normal lee cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Claud2010-2203_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Claud2010-2203-56">[56]</a></sup> At 0600Â <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on 26Â September, <a href="/wiki/European_Centre_for_Medium-Range_Weather_Forecasts" title="European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts">European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF)</a> model analyses indicated the existence of two <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure areas</a> along the shoreline of Italy, one on the west coast, sweeping eastward across the <a href="/wiki/Tyrrhenian_Sea" title="Tyrrhenian Sea">Tyrrhenian Sea</a>, while the other, slightly more intense, low was located over the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup> As the latter low approached the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Sicily" title="Strait of Sicily">Strait of Sicily</a>, it met an eastward-moving convection-producing <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, resulting in significant intensification, while the system simultaneously reduced in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Claud2010-2203_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Claud2010-2203-56">[56]</a></sup> It then achieved a minimum <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> of approximately 986Â <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.12Â <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) after transiting north-northeastward across the 40Â km (25Â mi)-wide <a href="/wiki/Salento" title="Salento">Salentine peninsula</a> in the course of roughly 30Â minutes at 0915Â UTC the same day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wind gusts surpassing 144Â km/h (89Â mph) were recorded as it passed over Salento due to a steep <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">pressure gradient</a> associated with it, confirmed by regional <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">radar observations</a> denoting the presence of a clear <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup> The high winds inflicted moderate damages throughout the peninsula, though specific damage is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282-51">[51]</a></sup> Around 1000Â UTC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-3"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup> both radar and satellite recorded the system's entry into the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a> and its gradual northwestward curve back toward the Italian coast. By 1700Â UTC, the cyclone made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">Apulia</a> while maintaining its intensity, with a minimum atmospheric pressure at 988Â mbar (29.18Â inHg). The cyclone weakened while drifting further inland over the Italian mainland, eventually dissipating as it curved west-southwestward. A later study in 2008 evaluated that the cyclone possessed numerous characteristics seen in tropical cyclones elsewhere, with a spiral appearance, eye-like apparatus, rapid atmospheric pressure decreases in advance of landfall, and intense sustained winds, concentrated near the storm's eyewall;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4375_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4375-58">[58]</a></sup> the apparent eye-like structure in the cyclone, however, was ill-defined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2281_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2281-50">[50]</a></sup> Since then, the medicane has been the subject of significant study as a result of the availability of scientific observations and reports related to the cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-4"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup> In particular, the sensitivity of this cyclone to sea-surface temperatures,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miglietta2011_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2011-59">[59]</a></sup> initial conditions, the model,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davolio2009_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davolio2009-60">[60]</a></sup> and the parameterization schemes used in the simulations were analyzed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miglietta2015_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2015-61">[61]</a></sup> The relevance of different instability indices for the diagnosis and the prediction of these events were also studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conte2010_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conte2010-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="November_2011_(Rolf/01M)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="November_2011_.28Rolf.2F01M.29">November 2011 (Rolf/01M)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: November 2011 (Rolf/01M)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In NovemberÂ 2011, the first officially designated Mediterranean tropical cyclone by the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA)</a> formed, christened as 01M by the <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Analysis_Branch" title="Satellite Analysis Branch">Satellite Analysis Branch</a> and given the name Rolf by the <a href="/wiki/Free_University_of_Berlin" title="Free University of Berlin">Free University of Berlin (FU Berlin)</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA-bulletins_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-bulletins-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FUBerlin_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FUBerlin-63">[63]</a></sup> despite the fact that no agency is officially responsible for monitoring tropical cyclone activity in the Mediterranean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCFAQ_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCFAQ-1">[1]</a></sup> On 4Â November 2011, a frontal system associated with another low-pressure area monitored by FU Berlin, designated Quinn, spawned a second low-pressure system inland near <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>, which was subsequently named Rolf by the university. An upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> on the European mainland stalled as it approached the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> before approaching and interacting with the low known as Rolf. Heavy rainfall consequently fell over regions of southern France and northwestern Italy, resulting in widespread <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a> and flooding. On 5Â November, Rolf slowed while stationed above the <a href="/wiki/Massif_Central" title="Massif Central">Massif Central</a>, maintaining a pressure of 1000Â <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.53Â <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>). A <a href="/wiki/Stationary_front" title="Stationary front">stationary front</a>, stationed between <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, approached Rolf the same day, with the cold front later encountering and become associated with Rolf, which would continue for several days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FUBerlin_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-FUBerlin-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6Â November, the cyclone drifted toward the Mediterranean from the southern shoreline of France, shrinking to a diameter of merely 150Â km (93Â mi) across. Slightly weakening, Rolf neared the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> on 7Â November, associated with two fronts producing heavy rain throughout Europe, before separating entirely and transitioning into a cut-off low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FUBerlin_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-FUBerlin-63">[63]</a></sup> The same day, the NOAA began monitoring the system, designating it as 01M, marking the first time it officially monitored a medicane. A distinct <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>-like feature developed while spiral banding and intense convection became evident. At its highest, the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a> classified the system as T3.0. Convection gradually decreased, and a misalignment of the mid- and upper- level centers was noted. The system continued to rapidly weaken on 9Â November, before advisories were halted on the system later that day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA-bulletins_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-bulletins-2">[2]</a></sup> and FU Berlin followed suit by 10Â November, removing the name Rolf from its weather maps and declaring its dissipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FUBerlin_63-3"><a href="#cite_note-FUBerlin-63">[63]</a></sup> The deep warm core of this cyclone persisted for a longer time compared to other tropical-like cyclones in the Mediterranean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miglietta2013-2404_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2013-2404-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="November_2014_(Qendresa)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="November_2014_.28Qendresa.29">November 2014 (Qendresa)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: November 2014 (Qendresa)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8000" data-file-width="6000" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg/220px-Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg/330px-Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg/440px-Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cyclone Qendresa on 7 November</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Qendresa" title="Cyclone Qendresa">Cyclone Qendresa</a></div>
<p>On 6 November 2014, the low-level circulation centre of Qendresa formed near <a href="/wiki/Kerkennah_Islands" title="Kerkennah Islands">Kerkennah Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FUB_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FUB-64">[64]</a></sup> As the system was moving north-northeastwards and combining with an upper-level low from <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> early on 7 November, the system <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">occluded</a> quickly and intensified dramatically with an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>-like feature, thanks to favourable conditions. Qendresa directly hit <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> when it had lost its <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">fronts</a> with a more well-defined eye, with ten-minute sustained winds at 110.9Â km/h (68.9Â mph) and the gust at 153.7Â km/h (95.5Â mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUMETSAT_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-EUMETSAT-65">[65]</a></sup> The central pressure was presumed to be 978 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a> (28.88 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>). Interacting with <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, the cyclone turned northeastwards and started to make an anticlockwise loop. On 8 November, Qendresa crossed <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Syracuse" title="Province of Syracuse">Syracuse</a> in the morning and then significantly weakened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FUB_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-FUB-64">[64]</a></sup> Turning southeastwards then moving eastwards,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Qendressa moved over <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, before dissipating over the island on November 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span id="October_2016_(90M)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="October_2016_.2890M.29">October 2016 (90M)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: October 2016 (90M)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4050" data-file-width="5400" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg/200px-90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg/300px-90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg/400px-90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
90M on 30 October 2016</div>

<p>On early 28 October 2016, 35Â mph (60Â km/h) winds began to form into an extratropical cyclone south of <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>, in the Ionian sea. It quickly intensified, attaining wind speeds of 50Â mph (80Â km/h) as it slowly moved to the west, causing high waves and minor damage to cars near the <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Maltese</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Valletta" title="Valletta">Valletta</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2016cyclone_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-2016cyclone-68">[68]</a></sup> weakening the following day and beginning to move eastwards. However, later that day it began to re-intensify, becoming a tropical storm on 31 October. After passing over <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> it then began to quickly weaken, with the storm degenerating into an extratropical low on 1 November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medicane2016_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-medicane2016-69">[69]</a></sup> Tropical Storm 90M was also nicknamed "<i>Medicane Trixi</i>" by some media outlets in Europe during its duration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trixi_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trixi-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_storms">Other storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Numerous other Mediterranean cyclones have occurred, but few have been as well-documented as those in 1969, 1982, 1995, 1996, 2006, 2011, and 2014. On 27Â September 1983, a medicane was observed at sea between <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, looping around <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, coming ashore twice on the islands, before making landfall at <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a> early on 2Â October and dissipating. The development of the system was not encouraged by <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinic</a> instability; rather, convection was incited by abnormally high <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> at the time of its formation. It also featured a definitive <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>, tall <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus clouds</a>, intense sustained winds, and a warm core. For most of its duration, it maintained a diameter of 200 to 300Â km (120 to 190Â mi), though it shrank just before landfall on <a href="/wiki/Ajaccio" title="Ajaccio">Ajaccio</a> to a diameter of 100Â km (62Â mi). Four other notable cyclones occurred in the late twentieth century, on 26Â MarchÂ 1983, 29â30Â DecemberÂ 1984, 30â31Â OctoberÂ 1997, and 5â8Â DecemberÂ 1997, all exhibiting characteristics closely resembling tropical cyclones elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-6"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>A study performed in 2013 revealed several additional storms and additional information on medicanes forming on 7Â AprilÂ 1984, 14Â DecemberÂ 1985, 5Â DecemberÂ 1991, 15Â JanuaryÂ 1995, 10Â DecemberÂ 1996, 26Â JanuaryÂ 1998, 19Â MarchÂ 1999, and 27Â MayÂ 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-4_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-4-71">[71]</a></sup> A second study, conducted in 2007, revealed additional storms forming on 18Â OctoberÂ 2003, 27Â OctoberÂ 2005, and 15Â DecemberÂ 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fita2007-45_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fita2007-45-72">[72]</a></sup> A third study revealed several other cyclones, of which developed on 13Â SeptemberÂ 1999, 10Â SeptemberÂ 2000, 9Â OctoberÂ 2000, 19Â SeptemberÂ 2004, 3Â November 2004, 17Â and 26Â OctoberÂ 2007, 4Â DecemberÂ 2008, 13Â AprilÂ 2012<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miglietta2013-2402_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2013-2402-27">[27]</a></sup> and 30 October 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-October_surprise_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-October_surprise-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
