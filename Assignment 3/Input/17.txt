<p>The <b>dry season</b> is a yearly period of low rainfall, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>. The weather in the tropics is dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rain_belt" title="Tropical rain belt">tropical rain belt</a>, which moves from the northern to the southern tropics and back over the course of the year. The tropical rain belt lies in the southern hemisphere roughly from October to March; during that time the northern tropics have a dry season with sparser <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, and days are typically sunny throughout. From April to September, the rain belt lies in the northern hemisphere, and the southern tropics have their dry season. Under the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climates</a>, a dry season month is defined as a month when average precipitation is below 60 millimetres (2.4 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The dry season has low humidity, and some watering holes and rivers dry up. This lack of water (and hence of food) may force many grazing animals to migrate to more fertile spots. Examples of such animals are <a href="/wiki/Zebra" title="Zebra">zebras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Wildebeest" title="Wildebeest">wildebeest</a>. Because of the lack of water in the plants, bushfires are common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Data shows that in Africa the start of the dry season coincides with a rise in the cases of <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a>—which researchers believe might be attributed to the higher concentration of people in the dry season, as agricultural operations are all but impossible without irrigation. During this time, some farmers move into cities, creating hubs of higher population density, and allowing disease to spread more easily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rain belt reaches roughly as far north as the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and as far south as the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>. Near these latitudes, there is one wet season and one dry season annually. At the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> there are two wet and two dry seasons, as the rain belt passes over twice a year, once moving north and once moving south. Between the tropics and the equator, locations may experience a short wet and a long wet season; and a short dry and a long dry season. Local geography may substantially modify these climate patterns, however.</p>
<p>New data shows that in the seasonal parts of the <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon forest</a>, foliage growth and coverage varies between the dry and wet seasons—with about 25% more leaves and faster growth in the dry season. Researchers believe that the Amazon itself has an effect in bringing<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the onset of the wet season: by growing more foliage, it evaporates more water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, this growth appears only in the undisturbed parts of the Amazon basin, where researchers believe roots can reach deeper and gather more rain water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It has also been shown that <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> levels are much higher in the dry than in the wet season in the Amazon basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
