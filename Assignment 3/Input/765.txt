<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Eastern Shore of Maryland</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Maryland Eastern Shore" redirects here. For the university, see <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland_Eastern_Shore" title="University of Maryland Eastern Shore">University of Maryland Eastern Shore</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>Eastern Shore of Maryland</b> is a part of the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> that lies predominantly on the east side of the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> and consists of nine counties. As of the 2010 census, its population was 449,226, with just under 8 percent of Marylanders living in the region. The term "Eastern Shore" distinguishes a territorial part of the State of Maryland from the <a href="/wiki/Western_Shore_of_Maryland" title="Western Shore of Maryland">Western Shore of Maryland</a>, land west of the Chesapeake Bay.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CrisfieldWaterfront.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1680" data-file-width="2544" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/CrisfieldWaterfront.jpg/220px-CrisfieldWaterfront.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/CrisfieldWaterfront.jpg/330px-CrisfieldWaterfront.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/CrisfieldWaterfront.jpg/440px-CrisfieldWaterfront.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CrisfieldWaterfront.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Crisfield, a major seafood center along the Chesapeake Bay</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Eastern Shore consists of nine Maryland counties on the Chesapeake Bay's eastern side—or eastern side of the <a href="/wiki/Susquehanna_River" title="Susquehanna River">Susquehanna River</a> (the western border of Cecil County with Harford County. <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> borders across the <a href="/wiki/Mason%E2%80%93Dixon_line" title="Mason–Dixon line">Mason–Dixon line</a>), with Delaware to the east and north, the Atlantic Ocean on the east, and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Shore_of_Virginia" title="Eastern Shore of Virginia">Virginia's own Eastern Shore</a> on the south. Maryland's and Virginia's Eastern Shore and all of Delaware form the <a href="/wiki/Delmarva_Peninsula" title="Delmarva Peninsula">Delmarva Peninsula</a>.</p>
<p>The counties comprising the Eastern Shore are <a href="/wiki/Caroline_County,_Maryland" title="Caroline County, Maryland">Caroline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cecil_County,_Maryland" title="Cecil County, Maryland">Cecil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorchester_County,_Maryland" title="Dorchester County, Maryland">Dorchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent_County,_Maryland" title="Kent County, Maryland">Kent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne%27s_County,_Maryland" title="Queen Anne's County, Maryland">Queen Anne's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset_County,_Maryland" title="Somerset County, Maryland">Somerset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talbot_County,_Maryland" title="Talbot County, Maryland">Talbot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wicomico_County,_Maryland" title="Wicomico County, Maryland">Wicomico</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Worcester_County,_Maryland" title="Worcester County, Maryland">Worcester</a> counties. To the south, the Calvert-Scarborough Line separates Maryland from Virginia. A modern Worcester County highway map <a class="external text" href="http://www.roads.maryland.gov/Town_Gridmaps/100000_Worcester.pdf" rel="nofollow">(PDF)</a> shows this location. While comprising different boundaries than in the 17th-18th century, the Eastern Shore's geographic definition was set once everyone agreed on where Watkins Point—on the western side of the peninsula—is and where the Bay's shoreline began (since the bay side peters out into marshes and wetlands).</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In 1668, Philip [Calvert] obtained Virginia recognition of Maryland's claims to present-day Somerset County, surveying a dividing line between the two colonies with Surveyor General of Virginia, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Scarborough" title="Edmund Scarborough">Edmund Scarborough</a>. Meanwhile, he negotiated treaties with Lower Eastern Shore Indian tribes harassing English settlers. These treaties defined standards of conduct for Indian-English relations, establishing an overall peace in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The northern limit is harder to locate. Some dispute Cecil County as a true Shore territory due to <a href="/wiki/Interstate_95_in_Maryland" title="Interstate 95 in Maryland">I-95</a>'s presence with its surrounding developments, proximity to and influence from nearby urban areas like <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_Delaware" title="Wilmington, Delaware">Wilmington</a>, and a position straddling the <a href="/wiki/Elk_River_(Maryland)" title="Elk River (Maryland)">Elk River</a>–leaving 50% west of the Shore.</p>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/New_Castle_County,_Delaware" title="New Castle County, Delaware">New Castle County, Delaware</a>, Cecil County is crossed by the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Seaboard_fall_line" title="Atlantic Seaboard fall line">fall line</a>, a geologic division where the rockier highlands of the <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_(United_States)" title="Piedmont (United States)">Piedmont</a> region meet the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_coastal_plain" title="Atlantic coastal plain">Atlantic coastal plain</a>, a flat, sandy area that forms the coast. The coastal plain includes the <a href="/wiki/Delmarva_Peninsula" title="Delmarva Peninsula">Delmarva Peninsula</a> and hence the Eastern Shore of Maryland. The geology of Delmarva is an inseparable part of the Eastern Shore, which has few rocky outcrops south of Kent County.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chesapeake_and_Delaware_Canal" title="Chesapeake and Delaware Canal">Chesapeake and Delaware Canal</a> crosses from Back Creek on the Elk River to <a href="/wiki/Port_Penn,_Delaware" title="Port Penn, Delaware">Port Penn, Delaware</a>. While it was a shallow canal with locks after its construction in 1829, it was deepened in the early 20th century to sea level, and physically separates the Delmarva Peninsula from the rest of the United States. Maryland south of the canal is considered the Eastern Shore by residents. The term <i><a href="/wiki/Western_Shore_of_Maryland" title="Western Shore of Maryland">Western Shore</a></i> is used by Eastern Shore residents to describe all the counties of Maryland west of the Chesapeake Bay, but especially those of the Baltimore-Washington metropolitan area and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Maryland" title="Southern Maryland">Southern Maryland</a>.</p>

Blackwater National Wildlife Refuge</div>

<p>The north-south section of the <a href="/wiki/Mason%E2%80%93Dixon_line" title="Mason–Dixon line">Mason–Dixon line</a> forms the border between Maryland and Delaware. The border was originally marked every mile by a stone, and every five miles by a "crownstone". The line is not quite due north and south, but is as straight as survey methods of the 1760s could make it. It was surveyed as a compromise solution to a century-long wrangle between the Penn and Calvert families of England. If the Chesapeake Bay/Delaware Bay watershed divide had been taken as the borderline, Delaware would be about half its current size.</p>
<p>Finally, although this has received less attention than other parts of Eastern Shore culture, commercial east-west ties between Delaware towns and Maryland towns were culturally significant in Colonial and Early American periods despite the border line (which largely cut through woods and swamps). Trade with Philadelphia was conducted by overland routes to Delaware towns like <a href="/wiki/Odessa,_Delaware" title="Odessa, Delaware">Odessa</a> (then called Cantwell's Bridge) and <a href="/wiki/Smyrna,_Delaware" title="Smyrna, Delaware">Smyrna</a> (then called Duck Creek). Agricultural products and milled grain were taken up the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> by "shallop men" in small vessels called <a href="/wiki/Shallop" title="Shallop">shallops</a>. These cultural connections continue to this day.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ocean_City,_Maryland" title="Ocean City, Maryland">Ocean City</a> is a modern resort on what was once called the "seaside" or "seaboard side." It is on a long north-south sandspit that is essentially a <a href="/wiki/Barrier_island" title="Barrier island">barrier island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Claiborne" title="William Claiborne">William Claiborne</a> was granted land (part of the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Colony of Virginia</a>) in 1629 and named the land "Kent County". In 1631, he sailed north up the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> from its south and west side to the area known today as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kent_Island,_Maryland" title="Kent Island, Maryland">Kent Island</a>. There he made a fortified settlement that is considered to be the first English settlement within the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Maryland" title="Province of Maryland">Province of Maryland</a>. <a href="/wiki/Talbot_County,_Maryland" title="Talbot County, Maryland">Talbot County</a> was formed in 1662. <a href="/wiki/Cecil_County,_Maryland" title="Cecil County, Maryland">Cecil County</a> was formed in 1674, by proclamation of the Governor, from eastern portions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore_County" title="Baltimore County">Baltimore County</a> and the northern portion of Kent County. <a href="/wiki/Wicomico_County,_Maryland" title="Wicomico County, Maryland">Wicomico County</a> was formed in 1867, as the 9th and last county, created from Somerset and Worcester counties.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_the_counties">Formation of the counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Formation of the counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>1642 <a href="/wiki/Kent_County,_Maryland" title="Kent County, Maryland">Kent County</a>-In 1642, the governor and council appointed commissioners for the Isle and County of Kent. This act appears to have led to the establishment of Kent County, name after <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> County, England.</li>
<li>1661 <a href="/wiki/Talbot_County,_Maryland" title="Talbot County, Maryland">Talbot County</a>- named for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grace_Talbot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grace Talbot (page does not exist)">Lady Grace Talbot</a>, the wife of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Talbot_(statesman)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert Talbot (statesman) (page does not exist)">Sir Robert Talbot</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Irish</a> statesman, and the sister of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cecilius_Calvert,_2nd_Baron_Baltimore" title="Cecilius Calvert, 2nd Baron Baltimore">Cecilius Calvert, 2nd Baron Baltimore</a>.</li>
<li>1666 <a href="/wiki/Somerset_County,_Maryland" title="Somerset County, Maryland">Somerset County</a>-named for Mary, Lady Somerset, the wife of Sir John Somerset and daughter of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Arundell,_1st_Baron_Arundell_of_Wardour" title="Thomas Arundell, 1st Baron Arundell of Wardour">Thomas Arundell, 1st Baron Arundell of Wardour</a>.</li>
<li>1669 <a href="/wiki/Dorchester_County,_Maryland" title="Dorchester County, Maryland">Dorchester County</a>-Named for the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Dorset" title="Earl of Dorset">Earl of Dorset</a>, a family friend of the Calverts (the founding family of the Maryland colony).</li>
<li>1674 <a href="/wiki/Cecil_County,_Maryland" title="Cecil County, Maryland">Cecil County</a>.</li>
<li>1706 <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne%27s_County,_Maryland" title="Queen Anne's County, Maryland">Queen Anne's County</a>- formed from northern parts of Talbot and southern portions of Kent. Name after <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne of Great Britain</a> who reigned when the county was established.</li>
<li>1742 <a href="/wiki/Worcester_County,_Maryland" title="Worcester County, Maryland">Worcester County</a>-named for an <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Worcester" title="Earl of Worcester">Earl of Worcester</a>.</li>
<li>1773 <a href="/wiki/Caroline_County,_Maryland" title="Caroline County, Maryland">Caroline County</a>- formed from parts of Dorchester and Queen Anne's counties. The county derives its name from Lady Caroline Eden, wife of Maryland's last colonial governor of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Maryland" title="Province of Maryland">Province of Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sir_Robert_Eden,_1st_Baronet,_of_Maryland" title="Sir Robert Eden, 1st Baronet, of Maryland">Robert Eden</a>.</li>
<li>1867 <a href="/wiki/Wicomico_County,_Maryland" title="Wicomico County, Maryland">Wicomico County</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chesapeake_Bay_Maritime_Museum.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="500" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Chesapeake_Bay_Maritime_Museum.JPG/220px-Chesapeake_Bay_Maritime_Museum.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Chesapeake_Bay_Maritime_Museum.JPG/330px-Chesapeake_Bay_Maritime_Museum.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Chesapeake_Bay_Maritime_Museum.JPG/440px-Chesapeake_Bay_Maritime_Museum.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><a href="/wiki/Ocean_City,_Maryland" title="Ocean City, Maryland">Ocean City</a> was founded on July 4, 1875,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> when the Atlantic Hotel opened on <a href="/wiki/Assateague_Island" title="Assateague Island">Assateague Island</a>. At the time, Assateague Island was continuous from the Delaware state line to well south of Ocean City: the Ocean City Inlet was not formed until <a href="/wiki/1933_Chesapeake%E2%80%93Potomac_hurricane" title="1933 Chesapeake–Potomac hurricane">a hurricane in August 1933</a> cut across the south end of the town, although the inlet was cut not by waves sweeping inland, but by 4 or 5 days' worth of freshwater runoff from the coastal creeks running seaward. By 1935, government money had built <a href="/wiki/Jetty" title="Jetty">jetties</a> to make the inlet permanent, dividing <a href="/wiki/Fenwick_Island_(Delaware%E2%80%93Maryland)" title="Fenwick Island (Delaware–Maryland)">Fenwick Island (north)</a> from Assateague Island (south). Early transportation to the island was by train.</p>
<p>Until the 1820s, travel and commerce between the Eastern Shore and Baltimore were less important than the connections between it and Philadelphia. Water travel by sailboat and steamer linked the Eastern Shore to Baltimore more tightly beginning about 1813, when the first steamboat traveled the Bay. By the 1880s, railroad lines linked the Eastern Shore to Philadelphia and later, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>, by way of a railroad line straight south from <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_Delaware" title="Wilmington, Delaware">Wilmington</a> to <a href="/wiki/Dover,_Delaware" title="Dover, Delaware">Dover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delmar,_Maryland" title="Delmar, Maryland">Delmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_Maryland" title="Salisbury, Maryland">Salisbury</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Charles,_Virginia" title="Cape Charles, Virginia">Cape Charles</a>. Maryland's Eastern Shore was served by branch lines running generally southwest from the main route. See <a href="/wiki/List_of_railroad_lines_in_the_Delmarva_Peninsula" title="List of railroad lines in the Delmarva Peninsula">List of railroad lines in the Delmarva Peninsula</a>. The Eastern Shore's many branchlines were built after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> by local companies; eventually all were controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> (which also bought control of the steamboat and ferry routes), then <a href="/wiki/Conrail" title="Conrail">Conrail</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern" title="Norfolk Southern">Norfolk Southern</a>. Today the remaining active railroad tracks on the Eastern Shore are operated by short-line railroads <a href="/wiki/Bay_Coast_Railroad" title="Bay Coast Railroad">Bay Coast Railroad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delmarva_Central_Railroad" title="Delmarva Central Railroad">Delmarva Central Railroad</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Maryland_and_Delaware_Railroad" title="Maryland and Delaware Railroad">Maryland and Delaware Railroad</a>.</p>
<p>An east-west rail route ran from a ferry terminal at <a href="/wiki/Claiborne,_Maryland" title="Claiborne, Maryland">Claiborne</a>, west of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Michaels,_Maryland" title="Saint Michaels, Maryland">St. Michaels</a>, to Ocean City, via the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Baltimore_and_Eastern_Shore_Railroad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Baltimore and Eastern Shore Railroad (page does not exist)">Baltimore and Eastern Shore Railroad</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wicomico_and_Pocomoke_Railroad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wicomico and Pocomoke Railroad (page does not exist)">Wicomico and Pocomoke Railroad</a>. Travelers could also take a ferry to <a href="/wiki/Love_Point,_Maryland" title="Love Point, Maryland">Love Point</a> on Kent Island, board a <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne%27s_Railroad" title="Queen Anne's Railroad">Queen Anne's Railroad</a> train, and travel east to <a href="/wiki/Lewes,_Delaware" title="Lewes, Delaware">Lewes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rehoboth_Beach,_Delaware" title="Rehoboth Beach, Delaware">Rehoboth Beach, Delaware</a>.</p>
<p>Automobile transportation across the Chesapeake Bay was by ferryboat until 1952, when the first <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay_Bridge" title="Chesapeake Bay Bridge">Chesapeake Bay Bridge</a> was opened for traffic.</p>
<p>In the late 1950s and early 1960s developers began selling lots on Assateague Island, south of the inlet. However, <a href="/wiki/Ash_Wednesday_Storm_of_1962" title="Ash Wednesday Storm of 1962">a storm on March 6, 1962</a> destroyed houses, shacks, and roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The state and federal governments intervened before reconstruction by creating the <a href="/wiki/Assateague_Island_National_Seashore" title="Assateague Island National Seashore">Assateague Island National Seashore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assateague_State_Park" title="Assateague State Park">Assateague State Park</a>.</p>
<p>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Shore_Baseball_League" title="Eastern Shore Baseball League">Eastern Shore Baseball League</a> operated on three different occasions between 1922 and 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It was a Class D minor league with teams in all three states of Delmarva.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS_in_the_United_States" title="HIV/AIDS in the United States">epidemic of HIV/AIDS in the United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_City_Health_Department" title="Baltimore City Health Department">Baltimore City Health Department</a> official Robert Mehl persuaded the city's mayor to form a committee to address food problems; the Baltimore-based charity <a href="/wiki/Moveable_Feast_(organization)" title="Moveable Feast (organization)">Moveable Feast</a> grew out of this initiative in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fuller_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-fuller-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-godsend_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-godsend-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">[8]</a></sup> By 2010, the organization's region of service had expanded from merely Baltimore to include all of the Eastern Shore of Maryland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaypride_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gaypride-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the Eastern Shore comprises more than a third of Maryland's land area, it has a population of 420,792 (2004 census estimate), about 8% of Maryland's population.</p>
<p>The most populous city on the Eastern Shore is <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_Maryland" title="Salisbury, Maryland">Salisbury</a>, and the most populous county is <a href="/wiki/Wicomico_County,_Maryland" title="Wicomico County, Maryland">Wicomico County</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_metropolitan_area" title="Salisbury metropolitan area">Salisbury metropolitan area</a> is the only <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_statistical_area" title="Metropolitan statistical area">metropolitan statistical area</a>, comprising <a href="/wiki/Somerset_County,_Maryland" title="Somerset County, Maryland">Somerset</a>, Wicomico, and <a href="/wiki/Worcester_County,_Maryland" title="Worcester County, Maryland">Worcester</a> counties along with <a href="/wiki/Sussex_County,_Delaware" title="Sussex County, Delaware">Sussex County, Delaware</a>.</p>

<p>As a whole the Eastern Shore is considerably more conservative than the more-populated Western Shore. The region has a history of strongly supporting Republican candidates for governor. Republicans <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Sauerbrey" title="Ellen Sauerbrey">Ellen Sauerbrey</a> in 1994 and 1998, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Ehrlich" title="Bob Ehrlich">Bob Ehrlich</a> in 2002, 2006 and 2010 and <a href="/wiki/Larry_Hogan" title="Larry Hogan">Larry Hogan</a> in 2014 swept all nine counties.</p>
<p>At the presidential level, the Eastern Shore also leans Republican, although Kent and Somerset Counties have flipped back and forth for Democratic and Republican presidential candidates. The last Democrat to win Dorchester County was <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election_in_Maryland,_1996" title="United States presidential election in Maryland, 1996">1996</a>; Cecil County has not gone Democratic since <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> won the county in <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election_in_Maryland,_1976" title="United States presidential election in Maryland, 1976">1976</a>, whilst Queen Anne’s, Caroline, Talbot, Wicomico and Worcester Counties have voted Republican at every election subsequent to <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1964" title="United States presidential election, 1964">Lyndon Johnson’s landslide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Eastern Shore has long been a part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maryland%27s_1st_Congressional_district" title="Maryland's 1st Congressional district">Maryland's 1st Congressional district</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Democrat <a href="/wiki/Roy_Dyson" title="Roy Dyson">Roy Dyson</a> represented the 1st district from 1981 until he was defeated by Republican <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Gilchrest" title="Wayne Gilchrest">Wayne Gilchrest</a> in 1990. Gilchrest held the seat until 2008 when State Senator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrew_P._Harris" title="Andrew P. Harris">Andy Harris</a> defeated him in the Republican primary. Harris narrowly lost the subsequent general election to Democratic Queen Anne's County state's attorney <a href="/wiki/Frank_Kratovil" title="Frank Kratovil">Frank Kratovil</a>. In 2010, Harris again ran for the district and handily defeated Kratovil after a single term in office. Harris had held the seat without serious difficulty since.</p>
<p>In the Maryland General Assembly, the Eastern Shore encompasses a portion of district 35B and all of districts 35A, 36, 37A and 37B, 38A, 38B and 38C. All seats are held by Republicans except for state delegate in district 37A and state senator in district 38.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Marylanders visit Ocean City. The city composes a distinct culture of the Shore.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The skyline, containing tall hotels and condominiums, stands out within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delmarva" title="Delmarva">Delmarva</a>. On Ocean City's southern end a recreational <a href="/wiki/Boardwalk" title="Boardwalk">boardwalk</a> spans over thirty blocks, containing carnival rides and games, restaurants, bars, arcades, and clothing boutiques.</p>
<p>Tourists visit <a href="/wiki/Saint_Michaels,_Maryland" title="Saint Michaels, Maryland">St. Michaels</a> on a neck surrounded by water; the colonial former port of <a href="/wiki/Oxford,_Maryland" title="Oxford, Maryland">Oxford</a>; <a href="/wiki/Chestertown,_Maryland" title="Chestertown, Maryland">Chestertown</a>; and isolated <a href="/wiki/Smith_Island,_Maryland" title="Smith Island, Maryland">Smith Island</a> in the Chesapeake Bay. North of Crisfield is <a href="/wiki/Janes_Island_State_Park" title="Janes Island State Park">Janes Island State Park</a>, with camping and kayaking trails through marshlands.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing">Fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the southern end of the Chesapeake coast of Maryland, the town of <a href="/wiki/Crisfield,_Maryland" title="Crisfield, Maryland">Crisfield</a> is home to a fishing, crabbing, and seafood processing industry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main economic activities on the Eastern Shore are vegetable and grain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farming" title="Farming">farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a>, large-scale chicken breeding (<a href="/wiki/Perdue_Farms" title="Perdue Farms">Perdue Farms</a> was founded in <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_Maryland" title="Salisbury, Maryland">Salisbury</a> and is headquartered there today), and <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">Tobacco</a> was grown during colonial times. The agricultural economy switched to grain in the second quarter of the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (November 2011)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waterways">Waterways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Waterways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various waterways provide a medium for commerce and boaters: the Atlantic Ocean; <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Susquehanna_River" title="Susquehanna River">Susquehanna River</a>; and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chesapeake_and_Delaware_Canal" title="Chesapeake and Delaware Canal">Chesapeake and Delaware Canal</a>.</p>

<p>There are three major routes onto the Shore:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay_Bridge" title="Chesapeake Bay Bridge">Chesapeake Bay Bridge</a> spans 4.35 miles (7.00 km) of the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>, and at the time of construction in 1952, was the longest continuous over-water steel structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A second parallel span was added in 1973 and a third has been discussed, most recently in 2006. A third span will not open, according to state officials, until about 2025. The bridges made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kent_Island,_Maryland" title="Kent Island, Maryland">Kent Island</a>, site of the first English settlement on the Shore, into a bedroom community for Washington, <a href="/wiki/Annapolis,_Maryland" title="Annapolis, Maryland">Annapolis</a>, and Baltimore. Kent Island is part of Queen Anne's County.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_13_in_Maryland" title="U.S. Route 13 in Maryland">U.S. Route 13</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maryland_Route_213" title="Maryland Route 213">Maryland Route 213</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The two major highways on the Eastern Shore are U.S. Route 13 and <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_50_in_Maryland" title="U.S. Route 50 in Maryland">U.S. Route 50</a>, which meet in Salisbury.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Secession">Secession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Secession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eastern Shore has made several attempts to separate from Maryland. Proposals were debated in <a href="/wiki/Maryland_General_Assembly" title="Maryland General Assembly">Maryland's General Assembly</a> in 1833–1835, 1852, and 1998. There were earlier proposals visualizing a state encompassing the <a href="/wiki/Delmarva_Peninsula" title="Delmarva Peninsula">Delmarva Peninsula</a>. The 1998 proposal by state Senators <a href="/wiki/Richard_F._Colburn" title="Richard F. Colburn">Richard F. Colburn</a> and <a href="/wiki/J._Lowell_Stoltzfus" title="J. Lowell Stoltzfus">J. Lowell Stoltzfus</a> did not specify a status for Eastern Shore's nine counties following secession, but suggested the new state's name could be "Delmarva".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
