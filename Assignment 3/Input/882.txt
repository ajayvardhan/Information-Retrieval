<p><b>Tropical Storm Ana</b> was the first of two named <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Arlene" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Arlene</a> in 2017, on record in the North <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" title="Atlantic ocean">Atlantic basin</a> to exist in the month of April. The first tropical cyclone of the 2003 season, it developed as a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> from a non-tropical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low</a> on April 20 to the west of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>. It tracked east-southeastward and organized, and on April 21 it transitioned into a tropical cyclone with peak winds of 60 mph (95 km/h). Tropical Storm Ana turned east-northeastward, steadily weakening due to wind shear and an approaching <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, and on April 24 it became an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>. The storm brushed Bermuda with light rain, and the remnants produced precipitation in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. Swells generated by the storm capsized a boat along the <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> coastline, causing two fatalities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2003)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ana_2003_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Ana_2003_track.png/220px-Ana_2003_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Ana_2003_track.png/330px-Ana_2003_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Ana_2003_track.png/440px-Ana_2003_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ana_2003_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A non-tropical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure area</a> developed about 240 miles (390 km) south-southwest of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on April 18 through the interaction of an upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> and a surface frontal <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> The surface trough, which extended from the gale center to <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, brought a plume of moisture northward from the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> into the circulation, which caused heavy rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The non-tropical low tracked generally northward,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> with a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> to its east and west,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and on April 19 the system began producing sporadic convection near its center; early that day, satellite imagery indicated the presence of a tight inner core of winds. After turning to the northwest, it looped southeastward and gradually became separated from the surface frontal system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> due to the deepening of the upper-level trough over the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Convection became better organized over the center, and it is estimated the system developed into Subtropical Storm Ana early on April 20 while located about 250 miles (400 km) west of Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Operationally, the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> was not classified by the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> until 21 hours later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The subtropical storm tracked east-southeastward and continued to organize, and by late on April 20 an upper-level warm core was present over the system. Based on its organization, Ana is estimated to have become a tropical storm by 0000 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on April 21. Upon becoming a tropical storm, Ana attained a peak intensity of 60 mph (95 km/h), which was based on estimates from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hebert-Poteat_technique&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hebert-Poteat technique (page does not exist)">Hebert-Poteat technique</a> and data from <a href="/wiki/QuikSCAT" title="QuikSCAT">QuikSCAT</a>. Shortly thereafter, it made its closest point of approach to Bermuda, when it passed about 130 miles (210 km) southwest of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Operationally, the cyclone was first classified by the National Hurricane Center around this time, when it was considered a subtropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-5">[5]</a></sup> Strong upper-level <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> removed much of the convection,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc2-6">[6]</a></sup> though a small area of thunderstorms persisted near the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc3_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc3-7">[7]</a></sup> The storm became completely separated from the upper-level system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc4_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc4-8">[8]</a></sup> and the cyclone re-organized, developing an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye feature</a> late on April 21. Embedded within the flow of a cyclone to its north, Ana continued eastward,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc5_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc5-9">[9]</a></sup> and early on April 22 the wind shear again removed the convection from the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc6_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc6-10">[10]</a></sup> Convection waxed and waned throughout the day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc9_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc9-11">[11]</a></sup> and by April 23 the circulation had deteriorated in organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc10_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc10-12">[12]</a></sup> After turning to the northeast, the circulation center merged with an approaching <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> on April 24, and Tropical Storm Ana completed the transition into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>. The extratropical storm accelerated east-northeastward before losing its identity within the frontal zone on April 27 southeast of the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Impact,_records,_and_naming"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact.2C_records.2C_and_naming">Impact, records, and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2003)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact, records, and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Subtropical_Storm_Ana.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="720" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Subtropical_Storm_Ana.jpg/220px-Subtropical_Storm_Ana.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Subtropical_Storm_Ana.jpg/330px-Subtropical_Storm_Ana.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Subtropical_Storm_Ana.jpg/440px-Subtropical_Storm_Ana.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subtropical_Storm_Ana.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Subtropical Storm Ana in the western Atlantic Ocean on April 20</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to the development of Ana, the government of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> issued a gale warning for the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Upon its classification by the National Hurricane Center, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm warning</a> was issued for Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Meandering near the island for several days while developing, the storm dropped 2.63 inches (67 mm) of precipitation in a six-day period at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bermuda_International_Airport" title="Bermuda International Airport">Bermuda International Airport</a>. Winds on the island did not reach tropical storm force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Swells from the storm impacted the coast of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> The combination of the swells and an outgoing tide caused a boat to capsize in <a href="/wiki/Jupiter,_Florida" title="Jupiter, Florida">Jupiter Inlet</a> on April 20; two of its occupants drowned, and the other two were rescued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As an extratropical storm, the remnants of Ana dropped 0.87 inches (22 mm) of precipitation in the city of <a href="/wiki/Ponta_Delgada" title="Ponta Delgada">Ponta Delgada</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. Moisture from the remnants of Ana also produced beneficial rainfall in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Two ships recorded tropical storm force winds in association with Ana; the Atlantic Forest recorded 51 mph (82 km/h) and a pressure of 998 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mbar" title="Mbar">mbar</a> on April 22, and the Rosa Delmas reported winds of 47 mph (76 km/h) on April 23.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 20, Ana became the second <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> on record in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic basin</a> in the month of April, after a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1992_April_subtropical_storm" title="1992 April subtropical storm">subtropical storm</a> in 1992. After attaining tropical characteristics, it became the first tropical storm on record in the month of April, and was among the earliest forming tropical or subtropical cyclones in the Atlantic basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
