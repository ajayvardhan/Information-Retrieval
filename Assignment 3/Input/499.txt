<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Siberian High</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Siberian High</b> (also <b>Siberian Anticyclone</b>) is a massive collection of cold dry air that accumulates in the northeastern part of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> from September until April. It is usually centered on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variability_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-variability-1">[1]</a></sup> It reaches its greatest size and strength in the <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a> when the air temperature near the center of the <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure area</a> is often lower than −40 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (−40 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> is often above 1,040 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">millibars</a> (31 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>). The Siberian High is the strongest semi-permanent high in the northern hemisphere and is responsible for both the lowest temperature in the Northern Hemisphere, of −67.8 °C (−90.0 °F) on 15 January 1885 at <a href="/wiki/Verkhoyansk" title="Verkhoyansk">Verkhoyansk</a>, and the highest pressure, 1083.8 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibar" title="Millibar">mbar</a> (108.38 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilopascal" title="Kilopascal">kPa</a>, 32.01 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Agata,_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Agata, Russia (page does not exist)">Agata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krasnoyarsk_Krai" title="Krasnoyarsk Krai">Krasnoyarsk Krai</a> on 31 December 1968, ever recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Siberian High is responsible both for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_winter" title="Russian winter">severe winter cold</a> and attendant dry conditions with little snow and few or no glaciers across <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a>, the Siberian High is largely replaced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asiatic_low" title="Asiatic low">Asiatic low</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_High&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Siberian High affects the weather patterns in most parts of the Northern Hemisphere: its influence extends as far west as <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reconstructed_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-reconstructed-3">[3]</a></sup> and as far southeast as <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> where it is a critical component of the northeast monsoon. Occasionally a strong Siberian High can bring unusually cold weather into the tropics as far southeast as the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It may block or reduce the size of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-pressure_cell" title="Low-pressure cell">low-pressure cells</a> and generate dry weather across much of the Asian landscape with the exception of regions such as <a href="/wiki/Hokuriku_region" title="Hokuriku region">Hokuriku</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> that receive orographic rainfall from the winds it generates. As a result of the Siberian High, coastal winters in the main city of Pacific Russia <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a> are very cold in relation to its latitude and proximity to the ocean.</p>
<p>Siberian air is generally colder than Arctic air, because unlike Arctic air which forms over the <a href="/wiki/Polar_ice_cap" title="Polar ice cap">sea ice</a> around the North Pole, Siberian air forms over the cold tundra of Siberia, which does not radiate heat the same way the ice of the Arctic does.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genesis_and_variability">Genesis and variability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siberian_High&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genesis and variability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, the Siberian High-pressure system begins to build up at the end of August, reaches its peak in the winter, and remains strong until the end of April. Its genesis at the end of the Arctic summer is caused by the convergence of summer air flows being cooled over interior <a href="/wiki/North_Asia" title="North Asia">northeast Asia</a> as days shorten. In the process of the Siberian High's formation, the upper-level jet is transferred across northern Eurasia by adiabatic cooling and descending advection, which in extreme cases creates "<a href="/wiki/Cold-air_damming" title="Cold-air damming">cold domes</a>" that outbreak over warmer parts of <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>.</p>
<p>In spite of its immense influence on the weather experienced by a large proportion of the world’s population, scientific studies of the Siberian High have been late in coming, though variability of its behavior was observed as early as the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variability_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-variability-1">[1]</a></sup> However, recent studies of observed <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> over <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> have shown that weakening of the Siberian High is a prime driver of warmer winters in almost all of inland extra-tropical Asia and even over most parts of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variability_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-variability-1">[1]</a></sup> with the strongest relationship over the <a href="/wiki/West_Siberian_Plain" title="West Siberian Plain">West Siberian Plain</a> and significant relationships as far west as <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and as far southeast as <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>. <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">Precipitation</a> has also been found to be similarly inversely related to the mean central pressure of the Siberian High over almost all of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> during the boreal winter, and similar relationships are found in southern China, whilst the opposite correlation exists over the <a href="/wiki/Coromandel_Coast" title="Coromandel Coast">Coromandel Coast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>. Other studies have suggested that the strength of the Siberian High shows an inverse correlation with the high-pressure systems over North Africa. Another correlation has been noted, a connection of a weaker Siberian High and <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_oscillation" title="Antarctic oscillation">Antarctic oscillation</a> (AAO) is stronger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
