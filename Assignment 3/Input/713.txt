<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Santee River</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

Man-made Lake Marion was <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dammed</a> in the thirties, creating the lake in the middle of the rolling farmlands, forests, and <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> of the Santee River <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_valley" title="River valley">valley</a>. The upper river terminates in the lake today near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santee,_SC" title="Santee, SC">Santee, SC</a>. The lake has shallow and medium-depth habitats, creating a fertile environment for water-dependent species. Lake Marion is famous for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Awards" title="Awards">trophy quality</a> game fish, especially both <b><a href="/wiki/Bass_(fish)" title="Bass (fish)">small and large mouth bass</a></b>.</div>

<p>The <b>Santee River</b> is a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, 143 miles (230 km) long. The Santee and its tributaries provide the principal drainage for the coastal areas of southeastern South Carolina and navigation for the central <a href="/wiki/Coastal_plain" title="Coastal plain">coastal plain</a> of South Carolina, emptying into the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> approximately 440 miles (708 km) from its farthest headwater on the <a href="/wiki/Catawba_River" title="Catawba River">Catawba River</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. The Santee River is the second largest river on the eastern coast of the United States, second only to the <a href="/wiki/Susquehanna_River" title="Susquehanna River">Susquehanna River</a> in drainage area and flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Much of the upper river is impounded by the expansive, horn-shaped <a href="/wiki/Lake_Marion_(South_Carolina)" title="Lake Marion (South Carolina)">Lake Marion</a> reservoir, formed by the 8-mile (13 km)-long Santee Dam. It was built during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> of the 1930s as a <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">Works Progress Administration</a> (WPA) project to provide a major source of <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric power</a> for the state of South Carolina.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santee_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Santeerivermap.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Santeerivermap.png/300px-Santeerivermap.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Santeerivermap.png/450px-Santeerivermap.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9d/Santeerivermap.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Santeerivermap.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the Santee River watershed.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Santee is formed in central South Carolina 25 miles (40 km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_South_Carolina" title="Columbia, South Carolina">Columbia</a> by the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Wateree_River" title="Wateree River">Wateree</a> and <a href="/wiki/Congaree_River" title="Congaree River">Congaree</a> rivers. It flows southeast for 5 miles (8 km) before entering the northwest corner of Lake Marion, which stretches in a long wide arc to the southeast for approximately 30 miles (48 km) to Santee Dam. A navigable diversion canal first built in the 1970s at the southern tip of the lake connects to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Moultrie" title="Lake Moultrie">Lake Moultrie</a>, a reservoir on the nearby <a href="/wiki/Cooper_River_(South_Carolina)" title="Cooper River (South Carolina)">Cooper River</a>. The modern canal is operated by <a href="/wiki/Santee_Cooper" title="Santee Cooper">Santee Cooper</a> as part of the larger hydroelectric project on both rivers. The dam was finished in 1941.</p>
<p>Downstream from the reservoir it flows east, then southeast, forming the northeast boundary of <a href="/wiki/Francis_Marion_National_Forest" title="Francis Marion National Forest">Francis Marion National Forest</a>. Approximately 10 miles (16 km) from its mouth it <a href="/wiki/Distributary" title="Distributary">bifurcates</a> into two channels, called the <b>North Santee</b> and <b>South Santee</b>, that flow parallel and separated by approximately 2 miles (3 km), creating <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Island,_South_Carolina" title="Cedar Island, South Carolina">Cedar Island</a>. The two channels reach the ocean at Santee Point, approximately 15 miles (24 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Georgetown,_South_Carolina" title="Georgetown, South Carolina">Georgetown</a>, and not far from the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Pee_Dee_River" title="Pee Dee River">Pee Dee River</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santee_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wee_Tee_Lake_(oxbow_of_the_Santee_River,_South_Carolina)_(30_October_2005).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1172" data-file-width="1800" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Wee_Tee_Lake_%28oxbow_of_the_Santee_River%2C_South_Carolina%29_%2830_October_2005%29.jpg/300px-Wee_Tee_Lake_%28oxbow_of_the_Santee_River%2C_South_Carolina%29_%2830_October_2005%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Wee_Tee_Lake_%28oxbow_of_the_Santee_River%2C_South_Carolina%29_%2830_October_2005%29.jpg/450px-Wee_Tee_Lake_%28oxbow_of_the_Santee_River%2C_South_Carolina%29_%2830_October_2005%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Wee_Tee_Lake_%28oxbow_of_the_Santee_River%2C_South_Carolina%29_%2830_October_2005%29.jpg/600px-Wee_Tee_Lake_%28oxbow_of_the_Santee_River%2C_South_Carolina%29_%2830_October_2005%29.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wee_Tee_Lake_(oxbow_of_the_Santee_River,_South_Carolina)_(30_October_2005).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The river was named by early English settlers after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santee_tribe" title="Santee tribe">Santee tribe</a>, which inhabited areas on the middle part of the river. The first European contact was by a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a> party led by <a href="/wiki/Lucas_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Ayll%C3%B3n" title="Lucas Vázquez de Ayllón">Lucas Vázquez de Ayllón</a> in 1526. The Spaniards called the river the <i>Jordan</i> in honor of the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_River" title="Jordan River">Jordan River</a>.</p>
<p>After suffering a defeat by the English and their allies during the <a href="/wiki/Yamasee_War" title="Yamasee War">Yamasee War</a> in 1715–1716, the Santee were relocated. Many were shipped as <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slaves</a> to the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, opening up the river for British settlement as part of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Carolina" title="Province of Carolina">Carolina Colony</a>. Most of the Siouan peoples had migrated into the upper Midwest before European encounter.</p>
<p>In the late 18th century, the upper river was the site of the homestead of <a href="/wiki/Francis_Marion" title="Francis Marion">Francis Marion</a>, a patriot of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>. The original site of his homestead has been flooded by Lake Marion, which is named in his honor.</p>
<p>Construction of the 22-mile (35 km)-long <a href="/wiki/Santee_Canal" title="Santee Canal">Santee Canal</a>, linking the river to the Cooper, was begun in 1793 and finished in 1800. It allowed direct water transportation between the Upcountry of central South Carolina and Charleston, at the mouth of the Cooper and the harbor. The canal operated for 50 years before being made obsolete by the introduction of railroads.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, the state of South Carolina created the <a href="/wiki/Santee_Cooper" title="Santee Cooper">Santee Cooper</a> power utility. The main source of electric power for the utility came through federal construction during the administration of President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> of a hydroelectric project inland from Charleston. Starting in 1939, the Santee River was dammed, forming lakes Marion and Moultrie, and diverting the river's flow into the <a href="/wiki/Cooper_River_(South_Carolina)" title="Cooper River (South Carolina)">Cooper River</a> through a hydroelectric plant at Pinopolis. The WPA project was completed in 1941.</p>
<p>Though the project succeeded in providing cheap electricity to modernize rural <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unintended_consequence" title="Unintended consequence">unintended consequences</a> were changes to the character of both the Cooper and Santee rivers below the project. Deprived of most its water flow, the Santee River became more saline and its ecosystem gradually changed below the dam. The Cooper River received more of the freshwater and sediment loads that used to flow into the Santee and carried them downstream. This has resulted in greatly increasing the dredging costs to keep Charleston Harbor operating as a port. In the 1980s, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">Army Corps of Engineers</a> built a diversion canal to send some water back into the Santee, partially mitigating this problem.</p>
