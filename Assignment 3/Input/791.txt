<p><b>Hurricane Ida</b> was the strongest <a href="/wiki/Landfall" title="Landfall">landfalling</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> during the <a href="/wiki/2009_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2009 Atlantic hurricane season">2009 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, crossing the coastline of <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> with winds of 80 mph (130 km/h). The remnants of the storm became a powerful <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor'easter">nor'easter</a> that caused widespread damage along coastal areas of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_States" title="Mid-Atlantic States">Mid-Atlantic States</a>. Ida formed on November 4 in the southwestern Caribbean, and within 24 hours struck the Nicaragua coast with winds of 80 mph (130 km/h). It weakened significantly over land, although it restrengthened in the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Channel" title="Yucatán Channel">Yucatán Channel</a> to peak winds of 105 mph (165 km/h). Hurricane Ida weakened and became an extratropical cyclone in the northern <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> before spreading across the southeastern United States. The remnants of Ida contributed to the formation of a <a href="/wiki/November_2009_nor%27easter" title="November 2009 nor'easter">nor'easter that significantly affected</a> the eastern coast of the United States.</p>
<p>Numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_watches_and_warnings" title="Tropical cyclone watches and warnings">watches and warnings</a> were issued during the hurricane's existence. Areas from <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> to <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> were affected by either the storm or the nor'easter low. In Nicaragua, nearly 3,000 people evacuated coastal areas ahead of the storm. More extensive evacuations in Mexico relocated over 100,000 residents and tourists. In the United States, several counties in Louisiana, Alabama and Florida declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> because of fear of significant damage from the storm. Officials issued voluntary evacuations and most schools and non-emergency offices in the region closed.</p>
<p>In Central America, Ida brought heavy rainfall to parts of Costa Rica, Nicaragua and Honduras. Several people were reported missing in Nicaragua, however post-storm reports denied these claims. Thousands of buildings collapsed or sustained damage and roughly 40,000 people were left homeless. Damages from Ida in Nicaragua amounted to at least 46 million <a href="/wiki/Nicaraguan_c%C3%B3rdoba" title="Nicaraguan córdoba">córdoba</a> ($2.12 million US$). Aside from heavy rainfall in Mexico and Cuba, little impact from Ida was reported in either country. In the United States, the remnants caused substantial damage, mainly in the Mid-Atlantic States. One person was killed by Ida after drowning in rough seas, while six others lost their lives in various incidents related to the nor'easter. Widespread heavy rainfall led to numerous reports of flash flooding in areas from Mississippi to Maine. Overall, the two systems caused nearly $300 million in damage throughout the country.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ida_2009_track.png"><img alt="Map of a track starting in the southern Caribbean Sea and extending northward into the Gulf of Mexico and the United States Gulf Coast. Points along the track indicate storm intensity by color and classification by shape." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="678" data-file-width="1024" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Ida_2009_track.png/220px-Ida_2009_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Ida_2009_track.png/330px-Ida_2009_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Ida_2009_track.png/440px-Ida_2009_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ida_2009_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hurricane Ida originated from a weak <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that reached the western Caribbean on November 1, 2009. By November 2, the system spawned an <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">area of low pressure</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> which moved very little over the following days. The low became increasingly organized within a favorable environment that allowed deep <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convection</a> to develop. By November 4, the low had become sufficiently organized for the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) to classify it as Tropical Depression Eleven. At this time, the tropical depression was situated just southwest of <a href="/wiki/San_Andr%C3%A9s_(island)" title="San Andrés (island)">San Andrés Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Convective <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">banding features</a> became increasingly defined throughout the day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and six hours after becoming a tropical depression, the system intensified into Tropical Storm Ida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Light <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> allowed Ida to quickly intensify as it slowly tracked towards the Nicaraguan coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Late on November 4, microwave satellite imagery depicted an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye-like</a> featured forming within the storm. The storm tracked west-northwestward in response to a weak <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> over the north-central Caribbean Sea and a weak <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> over the southwestern <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>; these features were also responsible for Ida's slow forward motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Early on November 5, the storm intensified into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SSHS#Category_1" title="SSHS">Category 1 hurricane</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a> as it passed near the <a href="/wiki/Corn_Islands" title="Corn Islands">Corn Islands</a>. At approximately 1117 UTC, the center of Ida made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near Rio Grande, Nicaragua, with winds of 80 mph (130 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc5_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc5-4">[4]</a></sup> After the hurricane moved inland, the high mountains of Nicaragua caused the convection associated with the hurricane to diminish, resulting in rapid weakening. Roughly 18 hours after landfall, Ida weakened to a tropical depression as it turned northward over <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Late on November 6, Ida re-emerged over water, entering the northwestern Caribbean Sea. Upon moving back over water, the storm quickly began to redevelop, with convection increasing around the center of circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Early on November 7, Ida restrengthened into a tropical storm as it tracked just west of due north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Very warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> ahead of the system would have allowed for substantial intensification; however, wind shear over the area quickly increased, resulting in modest strengthening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Later that day, the storm turned northwestward in response to a strong trough over <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and a mid-level ridge extending from the Southeast United States to <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As Ida neared the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Channel" title="Yucatán Channel">Yucatán Channel</a>, an eye redeveloped and the storm quickly intensified into a hurricane. By the morning of November 8, the storm had attained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SSHS#Category_2" title="SSHS">Category 2 status</a> with winds of 100 mph (155 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Late on November 8, Ida attained its peak intensity with winds of 105 mph (165 km/h) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 975 mbar (hPa; 28.79 inHg). Shortly thereafter, increasing wind shear and forward speed caused the storm to rapidly weaken to a tropical storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Only a small area of convection remained near the center by the morning of November 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Despite the strong shear, the storm quickly re-organized, attaining hurricane status for a third time during the afternoon. Based on readings from a nearby oil platform and reconnaissance data, it was determined that Ida attained its secondary peak intensity near the southeast coast of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> with winds of 85 mph (140 km/h). However, this intensification was short-lived as a combination of increasing wind shear and decreasing sea-surface temperatures induced weakening to a tropical storm within three hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the morning of November 10, all of Ida's convection appeared displaced to the northeast and the forward motion of the storm slowed substantially. Additionally, the storm had begun to undergo an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical transition</a> near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Gulf_Coast" title="United States Gulf Coast">United States Gulf Coast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Shortly before making landfall near <a href="/wiki/Dauphin_Island,_Alabama" title="Dauphin Island, Alabama">Dauphin Island, Alabama</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the storm completed its extratropical transition. Ida crossed <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Bay" title="Mobile Bay">Mobile Bay</a> shortly thereafter and maximum winds decreased below gale-force. After slowly tracking eastward for several hours, the surface circulation of Ida dissipated over the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>, late on November 10. However, energy from the storm led to the formation of a new low off the coast of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. This new low quickly intensified and became <a href="/wiki/November_2009_nor%27easter" title="November 2009 nor'easter">a powerful nor'easter</a> that caused substantial damage throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_States" title="Mid-Atlantic States">Mid-Atlantic States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> By November 12, the system attained a minimum pressure of 992 mbar (hPa; 29.29 inHg) along with winds of 65 mph (100 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAIda_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAIda-11">[11]</a></sup> The extratropical low weakened the following day and moved out to sea after stalling along the North Carolina Coastline. The remnants of the cyclone persisted through November 17, by which time it had moved over <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPCIda_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPCIda-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>Shortly after being designated as Tropical Storm Ida on November 4, the government of Nicaragua issued a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm warning</a> for the entire coastline of Nicaragua, and the government of Columbia also issued a warning for the nearby islands of <a href="/wiki/Archipelago_of_San_Andr%C3%A9s,_Providencia_and_Santa_Catalina" title="Archipelago of San Andrés, Providencia and Santa Catalina">San Andrés and Providencia</a>. Later that day, a hurricane watch was declared for areas between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bluefields,_Nicaragua" title="Bluefields, Nicaragua">Bluefields</a> and the Nicaragua–Honduras border. As Ida moved closer to land, the tropical storm warning for San Andrés and Providencia was discontinued. Several hours later, the tropical storm warning and hurricane watch were modified to cover areas south of the Nicaragua–Honduras border to <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Cabezas" title="Puerto Cabezas">Puerto Cabezas</a> and a hurricane warning was issued for areas south of Puerto Cabezas to Bluefields. After Ida made landfall in Nicaragua, all hurricane advisories were discontinued and replaced by a tropical storm warning. Shortly thereafter, a tropical storm watch was declared for areas along the Honduran coastline between <a href="/wiki/Lim%C3%B3n,_Honduras" title="Limón, Honduras">Limón</a> and the Nicaragua–Honduras border. However, all watches and warnings were discontinued once Ida weakened to a tropical depression on November 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout Nicaragua, officials evacuated roughly 3,000 people from areas prone to flash floods and landslides, as rainfall in excess of 20 in (510 mm) was expected to fall. About 1,100 of the evacuees were from Corn Island<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFP1-13">[13]</a></sup> and Little Corn Island where their homes were not expected to hold up to hurricane-force winds. In Bluefields, roughly 1,100 people were evacuated to shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP1-14">[14]</a></sup> Authorities began stockpiling supplies such as food, blankets and water that could supply 20,000 people after the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ET1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ET1-15">[15]</a></sup> Upon the formation of Ida, officials in Costa Rica placed most northern regions under a yellow alert. Personnel from the Costa Rican Red Cross were also placed on standby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In El Salvador, officials raised the disaster alert level to green, the lowest stage of alert, on November 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> As Ida neared the coastline of Nicaragua, officials in Honduras warned residents of the likelihood of heavy rainfall from the storm. In response to this, the country's disaster alert level was raised to yellow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>On November 7, Tropical Depression Ida re-entered the Caribbean Sea and restrengthened into a tropical storm, prompting the NHC to issue a tropical storm watch for areas between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Felipe,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="San Felipe, Yucatán">San Felipe, Yucatán</a> and <a href="/wiki/Punta_Allen" title="Punta Allen">Punta Allen</a> in Mexico as well as in <a href="/wiki/Pinar_del_R%C3%ADo_Province" title="Pinar del Río Province">Pinar del Río Province</a>, Cuba. Several hours later, the watches were upgraded to warnings and a new tropical storm warning was declared for <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a>. A tropical storm watch was also issued for <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_la_Juventud" title="Isla de la Juventud">Isla de la Juventud</a> and a hurricane watch for areas between <a href="/wiki/Tulum" title="Tulum">Tulum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cabo_Catoche" title="Cabo Catoche">Cabo Catoche</a>, Mexico. Early on November 8, the tropical storm warning and hurricane watch for Mexico were modified to include areas from Punta Allen to <a href="/wiki/Playa_del_Carmen" title="Playa del Carmen">Playa del Carmen</a> and Tulum to Playa del Carmen respectively. A hurricane warning was also declared for areas between Playa del Carmen and Cabo Catoche. Later that day, the tropical storm warning for Grand Cayman was discontinued as Ida moved away from the island. Early on November 9, all watches and warnings for Cuba and Mexico were discontinued as Ida moved into the Gulf of Mexico and towards the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Mexico, officials declared a yellow alert, moderate hazard, as Hurricane Ida neared the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> on November 9. Roughly 36,000 tourists and 1,500 residents were evacuated from coastal areas of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a>. The Mexican Navy was placed on standby to assist in relief efforts once the storm had passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Later that day, the alert was raised to red, the highest level, as hurricane-force winds and heavy rains threatened the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> A total of 95 shelters were opened in the state to house the evacuees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>As Hurricane Ida moved over the Yucatán Channel on November 8, the NHC issued a hurricane watch for areas between <a href="/wiki/Grand_Isle,_Louisiana" title="Grand Isle, Louisiana">Grand Isle, Louisiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico_Beach,_Florida" title="Mexico Beach, Florida">Mexico Beach, Florida</a>. As the storm moved closer to the states, a tropical storm warning was declared for areas between Grand Isle and <a href="/wiki/Pascagoula,_Mississippi" title="Pascagoula, Mississippi">Pascagoula, Mississippi</a>, as well as areas between <a href="/wiki/Indian_Pass,_Florida" title="Indian Pass, Florida">Indian Pass, Florida</a>, and the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Aucilla_River" title="Aucilla River">Aucilla River</a>. The hurricane watch was also modified to encompass a smaller area, between Grand Isle and Pascagoula. A hurricane warning was also issued from Pascagoula to Indian Pass. During the afternoon of November 9, all hurricane watches and warnings were discontinued and the tropical storm warning was modified to include areas between Grand Isle and the mouth of the Aucilla River. As Ida became extratropical, the NHC discontinued all watches and warnings on the storm on November 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the threat of large swells, several oil rigs along the Texas coastline were evacuated as a precautionary measure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Workers from the <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron Corporation</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anadarko_Petroleum_Corporation" title="Anadarko Petroleum Corporation">Anadarko Petroleum Corporation</a> were evacuated from offshore platforms while those working for <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">ConocoPhillips</a> and <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a> remained on site. The <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Offshore_Oil_Port" title="Louisiana Offshore Oil Port">Louisiana Offshore Oil Port</a> was also shut down on November 9 as a result of Ida's passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> As a result of the decreased oil production, the price of oil rose more than $1 to $78 per barrel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Among the rigs that were damaged was the <i><a href="/wiki/Transocean_Marianas" title="Transocean Marianas">Transocean Marianas</a></i> which was drilling the <a href="/wiki/Macondo_Prospect" title="Macondo Prospect">Macondo well</a>. That vessel would be replaced on the Macondo Well by the <a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon" title="Deepwater Horizon">Deepwater Horizon</a>, which caused the <a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon_oil_spill" title="Deepwater Horizon oil spill">Deepwater Horizon oil spill</a> in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waxman_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-waxman-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On November 8, <a href="/wiki/Lafourche_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Lafourche Parish, Louisiana">Lafourche Parish, Louisiana</a> president, Charlotte Randolph, declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> for the parish as the storm approached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Gulf_Coast" title="United States Gulf Coast">United States Gulf Coast</a>. Although no evacuations were issued, all schools and government offices were closed through November 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Voluntary evacuations were issued for residents in <a href="/wiki/Plaquemines_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Plaquemines Parish, Louisiana">Plaquemines Parish</a> along coastal areas. The Belle Chasse Auditorium was converted into a shelter to house evacuees for the duration of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Grand Isle mayor David Carmadelle issued voluntary evacuation orders for residents in <a href="/wiki/Recreational_vehicle" title="Recreational vehicle">recreational vehicles</a> and trailers on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Nearly 1,400 families still living in temporary <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">FEMA</a> homes in Louisiana, in the wake of Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Katrina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Rita</a>, were urged to stay at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-TG1-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_County,_Alabama" title="Baldwin County, Alabama">Baldwin County, Alabama</a>, a local state of emergency was declared on November 9 as Ida neared landfall. Voluntary evacuations were declared for residents living along coastal areas or in mobile homes. All government offices were closed until November 10 due to the storm. The Baldwin County Coliseum was converted into a shelter to house possible evacuees during the storm as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In Mississippi, officials advised residents to remain vigilant and discussed possible evacuations. Residents living near <a href="/wiki/Pensacola_Beach,_Florida" title="Pensacola Beach, Florida">Pensacola Beach, Florida</a>, and nearby <a href="/wiki/Perdido_Key,_Florida" title="Perdido Key, Florida">Perdido Key</a> were urged to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG1_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-TG1-34">[34]</a></sup> On November 8, emergency officials declared a state of emergency in <a href="/wiki/Escambia_County,_Florida" title="Escambia County, Florida">Escambia County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-escambia_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-escambia-36">[36]</a></sup> The following day, <a href="/wiki/Walton_County,_Florida" title="Walton County, Florida">Walton County</a> was also placed under a state of emergency ahead of Hurricane Ida's arrival. Voluntary evacuations were issued for residents in low-lying areas and all non-emergency offices were closed until November 10. The Freeport High School gymnasium was converted into a shelter to house evacuees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p>Throughout Nicaragua, rainfall produced by the storm was significantly less than anticipated according to satellite derived estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WU1_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-WU1-46">[46]</a></sup> Initial fears were that more than 15 in (380 mm) of rain would fall; however, most areas received less than 5 in (130 mm), especially further inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WU1_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-WU1-46">[46]</a></sup> A maximum of 9.1 in (230 mm) fell in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Cabezas" title="Puerto Cabezas">Puerto Cabezas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> while areas further inland received less than 8 in (200 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WU1_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-WU1-46">[46]</a></sup> The most severe damage took place in Karawala and Corn Island, near where the storm made landfall. There, roughly 80 percent of the structures were destroyed and over 2,000 ha (4,900 acres) of crops were lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP1-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KarawalaDamage_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-KarawalaDamage-47">[47]</a></sup> On Corn Island, 40 homes, 3 schools and a church were destroyed and the electrical and water grids were severely disrupted. Roughly 6,000 people from the municipalities of Sandy Bay, Karawala, Kukra Hilla, Laguna de Perlas, El Tortuguero and the mouth of the Rio Grande were evacuated to 54 shelters during the storm. Officials stated that 42 people along the Miskito Coast were unaccounted for as they refused to evacuate before the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ET1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ET1-15">[15]</a></sup> The day after Ida passed through, officials began to assess the full extent of the hurricane's damage. An estimated 40,000 people were left homeless throughout the country and one person was listed as missing. Mayors of severely affected towns reported that there were numerous injuries, missing persons and extensive property damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2-48">[48]</a></sup> In Nicaragua, there were no confirmed fatalities as a result of Ida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Damage from Ida in Nicaragua was estimated to be at least 46 million Nicaraguan córdoba ($2.12 million USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KarawalaDamage_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-KarawalaDamage-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> A total of 1,334 people were injured by the storm throughout the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Final damage assessments from the Nicaraguan Government for mainland Nicaragua were completed on November 12. A government report said that 283 homes were destroyed and 1,899 others damaged; 1,184 latrines were destroyed and 444 were damaged, and 476 wells were destroyed and 1,139 were damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>

<p>Shortly after the storm moved inland, 700 civil defense personnel were deployed to the affected region; however, due to damaged roads and poor travel conditions, they struggled to reach isolated regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ET1_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-ET1-15">[15]</a></sup> The Nicaraguan army supplied relief crews with four helicopters and two AN-2 aircraft for damage surveillance and search-and-rescue missions in the wake of Ida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP2_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP2-52">[52]</a></sup> The government of Nicaragua allocated roughly $4.4 million in relief funds for those affected by the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KarawalaDamage_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-KarawalaDamage-47">[47]</a></sup> Several agencies from the United Nations provided residents affected by the storm with relief supplies and donated disaster funds to the country. The United Nations Population Fund provided $49,000 in funds; the <a href="/wiki/World_Food_Programme" title="World Food Programme">World Food Programme</a> deployed several rescue vehicles and logistics teams; UNICEF also provided logistics assistance in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> OCHA provided $2 million in relief funds; the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Sweden" title="Government of Sweden">Government of Sweden</a> provided 400,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_kronor" title="Swedish kronor">Swedish kronor</a> ($55,946 USD) for sanitation and health supplies; the <a href="/wiki/International_Red_Cross_and_Red_Crescent_Movement" title="International Red Cross and Red Crescent Movement">Netherlands Red Cross</a> also donated 20,000 euros ($27,226 USD) for non-food items.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere_in_Central_America">Elsewhere in Central America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Elsewhere in Central America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/2009_El_Salvador_floods_and_mudslides" title="2009 El Salvador floods and mudslides">2009 El Salvador floods and mudslides</a></div>
<p>In Costa Rica, the outer bands of Ida brought torrential rainfall, triggering isolated landslides. One of these landslides damaged three homes, leading to officials evacuating five families. Homes near Los Diques de Cartago were flooded and the sewage system was damaged, resulting in overflow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Veraguas_Province" title="Veraguas Province">Veraguas Province</a>, Panama, severe flooding displaced more 400 people after 84 homes were inundated up to their roofs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> A flooding disaster that killed 124 people in El Salvador was initially attributed to Hurricane Ida, although the National Hurricane Center quickly affirmed that the event resulted from a separate tropical low-pressure system in the Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> After weakening to a tropical storm, Ida moved over <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, where widespread heavy rains fell. A maximum rainfall of 7.1 in (180 mm) was recorded in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Lempira" title="Puerto Lempira">Puerto Lempira</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-18"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> These rains caused some rivers in the country to swell, but none overflowed its banks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> In northern areas of Honduras, minor flooding and fallen trees were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Caribbean_2">Northern Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Northern Caribbean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Cuba, the outer bands of Ida produced widespread heavy rainfall across western areas of the country. A maximum rainfall amount of 12.5 in (320 mm) fell in Manuel Lazo, while nearby areas received between 7 and 9 in (180 and 230 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-19"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Strong winds, gusting up to 87 mph (140 km/h) in localized areas, accompanied the storm during its passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Several rivers were swollen due to the rains,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MexCubaPDF_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-MexCubaPDF-61">[61]</a></sup> including the <a href="/wiki/Cuyaguateje_River" title="Cuyaguateje River">Cuyaguateje River</a>, which overflowed its banks and flooded nearby areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In the Yucatán Peninsula, significantly less rain fell due to the asymmetrical structure of Ida even though the peninsula was relatively close to the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-20"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Isla_Holbox" title="Isla Holbox">Isla Holbox</a>, recorded substantial flooding, with roughly 70 percent of the island underwater. However, only minor damage was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Little to no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beach_erosion" title="Beach erosion">beach erosion</a> was sustained in coastal cities such as <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Cancún">Cancún</a>; however, over 50,000 tourists were evacuated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chetumal,_Quintana_Roo" title="Chetumal, Quintana Roo">Chetumal, Quintana Roo</a>, during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MexCubaPDF_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-MexCubaPDF-61">[61]</a></sup> The outer bands of Hurricane Ida also affected <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a>. Moderate rainfall and gusty winds were reported across the island, and waves along the beach were estimated at 6 ft (1.8 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

Rainfall from Hurricane Ida and the subsequent nor'easter in the United States</div>

<p>Ahead of Ida's arrival in the United States, a tight pressure gradient between the hurricane and a high pressure system over the southeastern states resulted in strong winds across southern Florida. These winds, reaching 45 mph (75 km/h) in gusts, caused moderate damage in parts of the state. Roughly 3,000 people were left without power in <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Miami-Dade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broward_County,_Florida" title="Broward County, Florida">Broward</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach_County,_Florida" title="Palm Beach County, Florida">Palm Beach</a> counties. Several trees were reported to have been downed and some uprooted. One car was struck by a broken tree limb during the event. Additionally, moderate beach erosion was reported in counties along the Gulf Coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLNCDC1_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FLNCDC1-65">[65]</a></sup> Rainfall from the system impacted Florida for two days, resulting in accumulations between 3 and 5 in (76 and 127 mm) in parts of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">panhandle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLNCDC2_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FLNCDC2-66">[66]</a></sup> A maximum rainfall of 5.41 in (137 mm) fell in <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Florida_Rain_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Florida_Rain-19">[19]</a></sup> Coastal and inland flooding resulted in numerous road closures and schools and non-governmental offices were closed on November 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLNCDC2_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-FLNCDC2-66">[66]</a></sup> Water rise along the coast was estimated between 3 and 5 ft (0.91 and 1.52 m) at the height of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLNCDC3_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FLNCDC3-67">[67]</a></sup> Following the storm, a local <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> was declared in <a href="/wiki/Wakulla_County,_Florida" title="Wakulla County, Florida">Wakulla County</a>. Throughout Florida, damage from the storm amounted to $265,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLNCDC1_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-FLNCDC1-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLNCDC2_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-FLNCDC2-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FLNCDC3_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-FLNCDC3-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Alabama, where Ida made landfall, heavy rains resulted in widespread flash flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-21"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> A maximum of 9.83 in (250 mm) of rain fell in Opelika during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPCGC_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-HPCGC-20">[20]</a></sup> Several roads in coastal counties were closed after being covered by high water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Heavy rains in central areas of the state also resulted in moderate flooding. In <a href="/wiki/Calhoun_County,_Alabama" title="Calhoun County, Alabama">Calhoun County</a>, a three-block area of Anniston was inundated by 2.5 ft (0.76 m) of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In addition to the storm's heavy rains, waves up to 20 ft (6.1 m) caused severe damage along coastal regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALNCDC1_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-ALNCDC1-71">[71]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> of 4.38 ft (1.34 m) was recorded at Bayou La Batre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The Gulf State Park Pier near <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Shores,_Alabama" title="Gulf Shores, Alabama">Gulf Shores</a>, recently re-opened after being destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a> in <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004</a>, was damaged. Damage from beach erosion and coastal resorts amounted to roughly $9 million in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALNCDC1_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-ALNCDC1-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before making landfall in Alabama, Hurricane Ida brushed southeastern Louisiana, bringing light to moderate rains and increased surf to the state. Offshore, one person drowned after attempting to assist a boat that let out a distress signal during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-22"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> The rough seas resulted in moderate to severe beach erosion that caused roughly 1,000 ft (300 m) of levee to collapse. The levee collapse led to minor flooding and threatened three homes. The storm cut a new pass through Elmer's Island to Grand Isle between 100 and 200 ft (30 and 61 m) wide. A maximum sustained wind of 62 mph (99 km/h) and a gust of 74 mph (119 km/h) was recorded at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The highest rainfall total was recorded in <a href="/wiki/Venice,_Louisiana" title="Venice, Louisiana">Venice</a> at 1.16 in (29 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPCGC_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-HPCGC-20">[20]</a></sup> Although not solely caused by Ida, high tides along the Texas coastline led to a few road closures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>

<p>Minor effects from Ida were also experienced in <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPCIda_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-HPCIda-12">[12]</a></sup> In Mississippi, 4.13 in (105 mm) of rain fell in <a href="/wiki/Waynesboro,_Mississippi" title="Waynesboro, Mississippi">Waynesboro</a>. Some flooding was reported in areas near the Alabama border while winds of up to 45 mph (75 km/h) brought down trees. Along the coast, the hurricane's storm surge was estimated at between 3 and 3.5 ft (0.91 and 1.07 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall from the storm affected much of Georgia, with a large swath of 3 to 5 in (76 to 127 mm) falling in northern parts of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPCIda_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-HPCIda-12">[12]</a></sup> A peak of 7.32 in (186 mm) was recorded in <a href="/wiki/Lithonia,_Georgia" title="Lithonia, Georgia">Lithonia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCSE_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-WPCSE-21">[21]</a></sup> Additionally, minor rains affected parts of eastern Tennessee,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPCIda_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-HPCIda-12">[12]</a></sup> totaling 4.11 in (104 mm) on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Le_Conte_(Tennessee)" title="Mount Le Conte (Tennessee)">Mount Le Conte</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCSE_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-WPCSE-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Nor'easter"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nor.27easter">Nor'easter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Ida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nor'easter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/November_2009_nor%27easter" title="November 2009 nor'easter">November 2009 nor'easter</a></div>
<p>Along the east coast of the United States, a <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor'easter">nor'easter</a> involving the remnants of Ida resulted in widespread damage along coastal areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-23"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> strong winds downed several trees loosened in saturated soil. In <a href="/wiki/Rockingham_County,_North_Carolina" title="Rockingham County, North Carolina">Rockingham County</a>, one person was killed after being struck by a branch while driving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Four homes were destroyed along the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a>, and over 500 others were damaged, leaving at least $5.8 million in losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Widespread coastal damage and flooding took place in Virginia, as rainfall exceeding 7 in (180 mm) fell in many places and large waves battered beaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPCIda_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-HPCIda-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-24"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> In some areas, roads were closed multiple times due to flooding. Minor damage was also reported as a few homes were inundated with up to 1 ft (0.30 m) of water. Some areas reported a storm surge comparable to that of Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gloria" title="Hurricane Gloria">Gloria</a> in <a href="/wiki/1985_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1985 Atlantic hurricane season">1985</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Isabel</a> in <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Damage from the storm in Virginia was estimated to be at least $38.8 million, of which $25 million was in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk</a> alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAIda_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASAIda-11">[11]</a></sup> In New York, one person drowned after being caught in rough seas off Rockaway Beach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Total beach losses in the state reached $8.2 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
