<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Arcus cloud</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>An <b>arcus cloud</b> is a low, horizontal <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> formation, usually appearing as an accessory cloud to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">cumulonimbus</a>. <b>Roll clouds</b> and <b>shelf clouds</b> are the two main types of arcus. Arcus clouds most frequently form along the leading edge or "gust fronts" of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">outflow</a>; some of the most dramatic arcus formations mark the gust fronts of <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derecho</a>-producing convective systems. Roll clouds also may arise in the absence of thunderstorms, forming along the shallow cold air currents of some <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea breeze</a> boundaries and <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shelf_cloud">Shelf cloud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Shelf cloud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>A <b>shelf cloud</b> is a low, horizontal, wedge-shaped arcus cloud. A shelf cloud is attached to the base of the parent <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a>, which is usually a thunderstorm, but could form on any type of convective clouds. Rising cloud motion often can be seen in the leading (outer) part of the shelf cloud, while the underside often appears <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent</a> and wind-torn. Cool, sinking air from a <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">storm cloud</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdraft" title="Downdraft">downdraft</a> spreads out across the land surface, with the leading edge called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gust_front" title="Gust front">gust front</a>. This outflow cuts under warm air being drawn into the storm's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updraft</a>. As the lower cooler air lifts the warm moist air, its water condenses, creating a cloud which often rolls with the different winds above and below (<a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>).</p>
<p>People seeing a shelf cloud may believe they have seen a <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">wall cloud</a>. This is likely a mistake, since an approaching shelf cloud appears to form a wall made of cloud. A shelf cloud usually appears on the leading edge of a storm, and a wall cloud will usually be at the rear of the storm.</p>
<p>A sharp, strong gust front will cause the lowest part of the leading edge of a shelf cloud to be ragged and lined with rising <a href="/wiki/Fractus_cloud" title="Fractus cloud">fractus clouds</a>. In a severe case there will be <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortices</a> along the edge, with twisting masses of <a href="/wiki/Scud_(cloud)" title="Scud (cloud)">scud</a> that may reach to the ground or be accompanied by rising dust. A very low shelf cloud accompanied by these signs is the best indicator that a potentially violent wind <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">squall</a> is approaching. An extreme example of this phenomenon looks almost like a tornado and is known as a <a href="/wiki/Gustnado" title="Gustnado">gustnado</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roll_cloud"><span id="volutus"></span><span id="Volutus"></span>Roll cloud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arcus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roll cloud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <b>roll cloud</b> (<i>Cloud Atlas</i> name <i><b>volutus</b></i>) is a low, horizontal, tube-shaped, and relatively rare type of arcus cloud. They differ from shelf clouds by being completely detached from other cloud features. Roll clouds usually appear to be "rolling" about a horizontal axis. They are a solitary wave called a <a href="/wiki/Soliton" title="Soliton">soliton</a>, which is a wave that has a single crest and moves without changing speed or shape. One of the most famous frequent occurrences is the <a href="/wiki/Morning_Glory_cloud" title="Morning Glory cloud">Morning Glory cloud</a> in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, which can occur up to four out of ten days in October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> One of the main causes of the Morning Glory cloud is the <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> circulation associated with sea breezes that develop over the <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">Cape York Peninsula</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">Gulf of Carpentaria</a>. However, similar features can be created by <a href="/wiki/Microburst" title="Microburst">downdrafts</a> from thunderstorms and are not exclusively associated with coastal regions.</p>
<p>Coastal roll clouds have been seen in many places, including <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" title="Shetland Islands">Shetland Islands</a>, the North Sea coast, coastal regions of Australia, and Nome, Alaska.</p>
