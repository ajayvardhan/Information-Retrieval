<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Simoom</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the desert wind. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Simoom_(disambiguation)" title="Simoom (disambiguation)">Simoom (disambiguation)</a> and <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Simoun_(disambiguation)" title="Simoun (disambiguation)">Simoun (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Simoom</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">سموم</span>‎‎ <i>samūm</i>; from the root <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">س م م</span> <i>s-m-m</i>, <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">سم</span> "to poison") is a strong, dry, dust-laden wind usually used to describe a <a href="/wiki/List_of_local_winds" title="List of local winds">local wind</a> that blows in the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_territories" title="Palestinian territories">Palestine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, and the deserts of <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>. Its temperature may exceed 54 °C (129 °F) and the humidity may fall below 10%. Alternative spellings include <b>samoon</b>, <b>samun</b>, <b>simoun</b>, and <b>simoon</b>. Another name used for this wind is <b>samiel</b> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> <i>samyeli</i>). Simoom winds have an alternative type occurring in the region of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> known as "Garmsil" (гармсель).</p>
<p>The name means "poison wind" and is given because the sudden onset of simoom may also cause <a href="/wiki/Heat_stroke" title="Heat stroke">heat stroke</a>. This is attributed to the fact that the hot wind brings more heat to the body than can be disposed of by the <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hyperthermia" title="Hyperthermia">perspiration</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Nuttall_Encyclop%C3%A6dia" title="The Nuttall Encyclopædia">The Nuttall Encyclopædia</a></i> described the simoom:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The storm moves in <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> (circular) form, carrying clouds of dust and sand, and produces on humans and animals a suffocating effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A 19th-century account of simoom in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> reads:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Egypt is also subject, particularly during the spring and summer, to the hot wind called the "samoom," which is still more oppressive than the <a href="/wiki/Khamsin" title="Khamsin">khamasin</a> winds, but of much shorter duration, seldom lasting longer than a quarter of an hour or twenty minutes. It generally proceeds from the south-east or south-south-east, and carries with it clouds of dust and sand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_North_America">In North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simoom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has been alleged that a "simoom" occurred on June 17, 1859 in <a href="/wiki/Goleta,_California" title="Goleta, California">Goleta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara, California</a>. For example, local historian Walker Tompkins wrote that during the morning, the temperature hovered around the normal 24 to 27 °C (75 to 81 °F), but around 1 p.m., strong super hot winds filled with dust began to blow from the direction of the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Ynez_Mountains" title="Santa Ynez Mountains">Santa Ynez Mountains</a> to the north. By 2 p.m., the temperature supposedly reached 56 °C (133 °F). This temperature was said to have been recorded by an official U.S. coastal survey vessel that was operating in the waters just offshore, in the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara_Channel" title="Santa Barbara Channel">Santa Barbara Channel</a>. At 5 p.m., the temperature had reportedly dropped to 50 °C (122 °F), and by 7 p.m., the temperature was back to a normal 25 °C (77 °F). Tompkins provided a supposed quote from a U.S. government report saying, "Calves, rabbits and cattle died on their feet. Fruit fell from trees to the ground scorched on the windward side; all vegetable gardens were ruined. A fisherman in a rowboat made it to the <a href="/wiki/Goleta_Beach" title="Goleta Beach">Goleta Sandspit</a> with his face and arms blistered as if he had been exposed to a blast furnace."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Also according to Tompkins, local inhabitants were saved from the heat by seeking shelter in the thick <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">adobe</a> walled houses that were the standard construction at the time.</p>
<p>However, experts contest this account. UCSB Professor Joel Michaelsen, for instance, said: “I have never found any outside source to validate Tompkins' story, and I am highly skeptical of its veracity. I don't doubt that strong hot, dry downslope winds could kick up lots of dust and produce very high temperatures - but in the 110 F - 115 F range at most. The 133 F just isn't physically reasonable, as it would require the creation of an extremely hot air mass somewhere to the northeast. Last Monday's weather was a very good strong example of the sort of conditions that would produce such a heat wave, and our temperatures topped out at least 20 degrees below Tompkins' figure. Stronger winds could have increased the heating a bit, but not nearly that much. Add to all that meteorologically-based skepticism Tompkins' well-known tendency to mix liberal doses of fiction into his 'histories,' and I think you have a strong case for discounting this one.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meteorologist Christopher C. Burt, writing on Weather Underground, says about the alleged incident: "There is no record of who made this measurement or exactly where it was made in Santa Barbara. Some later sources say it was made on a U.S. coastal geo-survey vessel. IF that is the case then the temperature is not possible since the waters off Santa Barbara in June are never warmer than about 70°F and any wind blowing over the ocean would have its temperature modified by the cool water no matter how hot the air. This report is singular and there is physical evidence (burnt crops and dead animals) that something amazing happened here this day, but the temperature record is impossible to validate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Figurative_use_of_the_word">Figurative use of the word</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simoom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Figurative use of the word">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>'s short story "<a href="/wiki/MS._Found_in_a_Bottle" title="MS. Found in a Bottle">MS. Found in a Bottle</a>" (1833) features a storm off the coast of Java, wherein "every appearance warranted me [the protagonist-narrator] in apprehending a Simoom."</p>
<p>In the political essay "Chartism," <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle" title="Thomas Carlyle">Thomas Carlyle</a> argues that even the poorest of men who have resigned themselves to misery and toil cannot resign themselves to injustice because they retain an innate sense that a higher (divine) justice must govern the world: "Force itself, the hopelessness of resistance, has doubtless a composing effect against inanimate Simooms, and much other infliction of the like sort, we have found it suffice to produce complete composure. Yet one would say a permanent Injustice even from an Infinite Power would prove unendurable by men."</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Walden" title="Walden">Walden</a></i> (1854), by <a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a>, references a simoom; he uses it to describe his urge to escape something most unwanted. "There is no odor so bad as that which arises from goodness tainted. It is human, it is divine, carrion. If I knew for a certainty that a man was coming to my house with the conscious design of doing me good, I should run for my life, as from that dry and parching wind of the African deserts called the simoom, which fills the mouth and nose and ears and eyes with dust till you are suffocated, for fear that I should get some of his good done to me—some of its virus mingled with my blood. No—in this case I would rather suffer evil the natural way."</p>
<p>In his 1854 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Hard_Times_(novel)" title="Hard Times (novel)">Hard Times</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a> in describing the oppressive midsummer heat of the sooty, smoky factories of Coketown, writes, "The atmosphere of those Fairy palaces was like the breath of the simoom; and their inhabitants, wasting with heat, toiled languidly in the desert" (book 2, chapter 1). In <i><a href="/wiki/American_Notes" title="American Notes">American Notes</a></i> Dickens also describes "that injurious [political] Party Spirit" as "the Simoom of America, sickening and blighting everything of wholesome life within its reach." (p. 93 in the 1913 Chapman &amp; Hall, Ltd. edition, online from Project Gutenberg).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bram_Stoker" title="Bram Stoker">Bram Stoker</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Dracula" title="Dracula">Dracula</a></i> (1897), Lucy, describing the appearance of Dracula in her room, writes in her journal entry on September 17 that "a whole myriad of little specks seemed to come blowing in through the broken window, and wheeling and circling round like the pillar of dust that travellers describe when there is a simoom in the desert."</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/A_Portrait_of_the_Artist_as_a_Young_Man" title="A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man">A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man</a></i> (1914), there is a reference to "Stephen's heart [withering] up like a flower of the desert that feels the simoom coming from afar."</p>
<p>In Sinclair Lewis' novel <i><a href="/wiki/Main_Street" title="Main Street">Main Street</a></i> (1920), there is a reference to "Aunt Bessie's simoom of questioning."</p>
<p>In keeping with its tradition of naming its aircraft engines after winds, the <a href="/wiki/Wright_Aeronautical" title="Wright Aeronautical">Wright Aeronautical</a> R-1200 of 1925 was called the Simoon.</p>
<p>A simoon strikes during chapter 2 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_serial" title="Film serial">film serial</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Tarzan_the_Tiger" title="Tarzan the Tiger">Tarzan the Tiger</a></i> (1929).</p>
<p>In <i>Making a President</i> (1932), <a href="/wiki/H._L._Mencken" title="H. L. Mencken">H. L. Mencken</a> refers to "a veritable simoon of hiccups."</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Patrick_O%27Brian" title="Patrick O'Brian">Patrick O'Brian</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Post_Captain_(novel)" title="Post Captain (novel)">Post Captain</a></i> (1972), Diana Villiers' mentally troubled cousin, Edward Lowndes, upon learning that Doctor Maturin is a naval surgeon, remarks, "Very good—you are <i>upon</i> the sea but not <i>in</i> it: you are not an advocate for cold baths. The sea, the sea! Where should we be without it? Frizzled to a mere toast, sir; parched, desiccated by the simoom, the dread simoom."</p>
<p>A song titled "Simoon" features on the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Magic_Orchestra" title="Yellow Magic Orchestra">Yellow Magic Orchestra</a>'s epoymously titled <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Magic_Orchestra_(album)" title="Yellow Magic Orchestra (album)">album</a> that was released in 1978. Also, <a href="/wiki/The_Creatures" title="The Creatures">The Creatures</a> have a song called "Simoom" on their 1989 album <i><a href="/wiki/Boomerang_(The_Creatures_album)" title="Boomerang (The Creatures album)">Boomerang</a></i>.</p>
<p>In the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_English_Patient_(film)" title="The English Patient (film)">The English Patient</a></i> (1996) there is a scene in which Count László Almásy regales Katharine Clifton with histories of named winds, one of them being the "Simoon." Alluding to the records of Herodotus, Almásy tells Katharine that there was once a certain Arabic people who deemed the "Simoon" so evil that they marched out to meet it ranked as an army, "their swords raised."</p>
<p>In the collectible card game <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magic:_the_Gathering" title="Magic: the Gathering">Magic: the Gathering</a>, card named "Simoon" first appeared in the Visions expansion set on a fictional continent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jamuraa" title="Jamuraa">Jamuraa</a>. This card saw play in the <a href="/wiki/Sideboard" title="Sideboard">sideboard</a> of contemporary Type II decks and was especially effective against the popular Five Colours Green decks that heavily relied on small creatures with toughness of 1.</p>
