<p>A <b>buoy</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɔɪ/: 'oi' in 'choice'">ɔɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>, also <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ɔɪ/: 'oi' in 'choice'">ɔɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a>:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">floating</a> device that can have many purposes. It can be <a href="/wiki/Anchor" title="Anchor">anchored</a> (stationary) or allowed to drift with ocean currents. The word, of <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> or <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">Middle Dutch</a> origin, is (in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>) now most commonly pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɔɪ/: 'oi' in 'choice'">ɔɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span> (identical with <a href="/wiki/Boy" title="Boy">boy</a>, as in <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyant</a>). In <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> the pronunciation is closer to "<i>boo-ee</i>."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sea_mark" title="Sea mark">Sea mark</a> – aids <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilotage" title="Pilotage">pilotage</a> by marking a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipping" title="Shipping">maritime</a> channel, hazard and administrative area to allow <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> to <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigate</a> safely. Some navigational buoys are fitted with a bell or gong, which sounds when waves move the buoy</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lifebuoy" title="Lifebuoy">Lifebuoy</a> – used as a life saving buoy designed to be thrown to a person in the water to provide <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>. Usually has a connecting line allowing the casualty to be pulled to the rescuer</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">Submarine</a> communication buoys – used for release in case of emergencies or for communication</li>
<li>DAN buoy – has several meanings:
<ul>
<li>A large maritime <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigational</a> aid providing a platform for <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> beacons</li>
<li>A lifebuoy with flags used on <a href="/wiki/Yacht" title="Yacht">yachts</a> and smaller pleasure craft</li>
<li>A temporary marker buoy used during <a href="/wiki/Seine_fishing#Danish_seine" title="Seine fishing">Danish seine</a> fishing to mark the anchor position of a net.</li>
<li>A temporary marker buoy set by <a href="/wiki/Danlayer" title="Danlayer">danlayers</a> during minesweeping operations to indicate the boundaries of swept paths, swept areas, known hazards, and other locations or reference points.</li>
<li>A temporary marker buoy set to mark a <a href="/wiki/Man_overboard" title="Man overboard">man overboard</a> position.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Large Navigational Buoy (LNB or <a href="/wiki/Lanby_buoy" title="Lanby buoy">Lanby buoy</a>) is an automatic buoy over 10m high equipped with a powerful light monitored electronically as a replacement for lightships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A LNB may be marked on charts as a "Superbuoy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sonobuoy" title="Sonobuoy">Sonobuoy</a> – used by <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine warfare</a> aircraft to detect <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a> by <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">SONAR</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Surface_marker_buoy" title="Surface marker buoy">Surface marker buoy</a> – taken on dives by <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba divers</a> to mark their position underwater<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decompression_buoy" title="Decompression buoy">Decompression buoy</a> – deployed by submerged scuba divers to mark their position underwater whilst doing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decompression_stops" title="Decompression stops">decompression stops</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diving_shot" title="Diving shot">Shot buoy</a> – used to mark dive sites for the boat safety cover of scuba divers so that the divers can descend to the dive site more easily in conditions of low visibility or <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tidal currents</a> and more safely do <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decompression_stops" title="Decompression stops">decompression stops</a> on their ascent.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Safe_water_mark" title="Safe water mark">Safe water mark</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairway_Buoy" title="Fairway Buoy">Fairway Buoy</a> – a navigational buoy which marks the entrance to a channel or a nearby landfall</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_Wreck_Buoys&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Emergency Wreck Buoys (page does not exist)">Emergency Wreck Buoys</a> – Emergency Wreck Buoys provide a clear and unambiguous means of marking new wrecks. This buoy is used as a temporary response, typically for the first 24–72 hours. This buoy is coloured in an equal number of blue and yellow vertical stripes and is fitted with an alternating blue and yellow flashing light. This has come about due to the collisions which occurred in the Dover Straits in 2002 when vessels struck the new wreck of the <a href="/wiki/MV_Tricolor" title="MV Tricolor">MV Tricolor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Navigational_aid#Lateral_marker" title="Navigational aid">lateral marker</a> buoy</li>
<li>Mooring buoys – used to keep one end of a <a href="/wiki/Mooring_(watercraft)" title="Mooring (watercraft)">mooring</a> cable or chain on the water's surface so that <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> or boats can tie on to it</li>
<li>Tripping buoys – used to keep one end of a 'tripping line' on the water's surface so that a stuck <a href="/wiki/Anchor" title="Anchor">anchor</a> can more easily be freed</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Weather_buoy" title="Weather buoy">Weather buoys</a> – equipped to measure weather parameters such as air temperature, barometric pressure, wind speed and direction and to report these data via satellite radio links such as the purpose-built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argos_System" title="Argos System">Argos System</a> or commercial <a href="/wiki/Satellite_phone" title="Satellite phone">satellite phone</a> networks to meteorological centres for use in forecasting and climate study. May be anchored (moored buoys) or allowed to drift (drifting buoys) in the open ocean currents. Position is calculated by the satellite. Weather buoys are sometimes referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_Data_Acquisition_System" title="Ocean Data Acquisition System">ODAS</a> buoys or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_Data_Acquisition_System" title="Ocean Data Acquisition System">Ocean Data Acquisition Systems</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and may be marked on charts as "Superbuoys."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Deep-ocean_Assessment_and_Reporting_of_Tsunamis" title="Deep-ocean Assessment and Reporting of Tsunamis">Tsunami buoys</a> – anchored buoys that can detect sudden changes in undersea water pressure are used as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsunami_warning_systems" title="Tsunami warning systems">tsunami warning systems</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Tsunami_Warning_Center" title="Pacific Tsunami Warning Center">Pacific Tsunami Warning Center</a> and Indian Oceans.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Spar_buoy" title="Spar buoy">Spar buoy</a> – a tall, thin buoy that floats upright in the water, e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R/P_FLIP" title="R/P FLIP">R/P FLIP</a>.</li>
<li>Profiling buoy – specialized models which adjust buoyancy so that they will sink at a controlled rate to 2,000 metres below the surface while measuring sea temperatures and salinity. After a time, typically 10 days, the buoy returns to the surface, transmits its data via satellite, and then sinks again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kery1989_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kery1989-6">[6]</a></sup> See <a href="/wiki/Argo_(oceanography)" title="Argo (oceanography)">Argo (oceanography)</a>.</li>
<li>Ice marking buoys – used for marking ice holes in frozen lakes and rivers, so that snowmobiles do not drive over the holes.</li>
<li>Marker buoys – used in <a href="/wiki/Naval_warfare" title="Naval warfare">naval warfare</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine warfare</a>, is a light-emitting or smoke-emitting, or both, marker using some kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic" title="Pyrotechnic">pyrotechnic</a> to provide the <a href="/wiki/Flare" title="Flare">flare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">smoke</a>. It is commonly a 3-inch (76 mm) diameter device about 20 inches (500 mm) long that is set off by contact with seawater and floats on the surface. Some markers extinguish after a set period and others are made to sink.</li>
<li>Lobster trap buoys – brightly colored buoys used for the marking of lobster trap locations so the person <a href="/wiki/Lobster_fishing" title="Lobster fishing">lobster fishing</a> can find their lobster traps. Each lobster fisherman has his or her own color markings or registration numbers so they know which ones are theirs. They are only allowed to haul their own traps and must display their buoy color or license number on their boat so law enforcement officials know what they should be hauling. The buoys are brightly colored with highly visible numbers so they can be seen under conditions when there is poor visibility like <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_smoke" title="Sea smoke">sea smoke</a>, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_buoy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wave buoy (page does not exist)">Wave buoy</a> – used to measure the movement of the water surface as a wave train. The wave train is analysed to determine statistics like the <a href="/wiki/Significant_wave_height" title="Significant wave height">significant wave height</a> and period, and wave direction.</li>
<li>Target buoy – used to simulate target (like small boat) in live fire exercise by naval and coastal forces, usually targeted by weapons (medium size) like HMG's, rapid fire cannons (20 or so mm), autocannons (bigger ones up to 40 and 57mm) and also anti-tank rockets.</li>
<li>Wreck buoy – a buoy to mark a wrecked ship to warn other ships to keep away because of unseen hazards.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Self-locating_datum_marker_buoy" title="Self-locating datum marker buoy">Self-locating datum marker buoy</a> (SLDMB) – A 70% scale Coastal Ocean Dynamics Experiment (CODE)/Davis-style oceanographic surface drifter with drogue vanes between 30 and 100 cm deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-METOCEAN_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-METOCEAN-9">[9]</a></sup> This particular surface drifter is designed specifically for deployment from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Coast_Guard" title="U.S. Coast Guard">U.S. Coast Guard</a> vessel or airframe for search and rescue. Since the SLDMB has a very small surface area above the ocean surface and a high underwater surface area, there is very little <a href="/wiki/Leeway" title="Leeway">leeway</a> in response to the direct forcing of winds and waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bang_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bang-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>The space buoy is a common element in science fiction that refers to a stationary object in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> that provides navigation data or warnings about that particular area.</li>
<li>Buoy racing is the most prevalent form of <a href="/wiki/Yacht_racing" title="Yacht racing">yacht racing</a></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buoy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The word "buoyed" can also be used figuratively. For example, a person can buoy ('lift up') someone's spirits by providing help and empathy.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_A._Stephen" title="George A. Stephen">George A. Stephen</a>, founder of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weber-Stephen_Products_Co." title="Weber-Stephen Products Co.">Weber-Stephen Products Co.</a>, invented the kettle grill by cutting a metal buoy in half and fashioning a <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">dome</a> shaped grill with a rounded lid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
