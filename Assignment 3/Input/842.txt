<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hurricane Hortense</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Hurricane Hortense</b> was the first hurricane to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hugo" title="Hurricane Hugo">Hurricane Hugo</a> in <a href="/wiki/1989_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1989 Atlantic hurricane season">1989</a>. The eighth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>, eighth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a>, and sixth hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/1996_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1996 Atlantic hurricane season">1996 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, Hortense developed on September 3 from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> in the central <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.</p>
<p>Initially a tropical depression, it headed westward without significant strengthening for four days due to unfavorable upper-level winds. While nearing the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> upper-level winds decreased, allowing the depression to become Tropical Storm Hortense on September 7. Hortense crossed <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> on September 8 and entered the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. By on the following day, it was upgraded to a hurricane while curving northwestward. Hortense made landfall in Puerto Rico on September 9 and brushed the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> shortly thereafter.</p>
<p>After re-entering the Atlantic, Hortense began to substantially strengthen and peaked as a 140 mph (220 km/h) Category 4 hurricane early on September 13. Thereafter, the storm steadily weakened as it tracked rapidly north-northeastward. Early on September 15, Hortense made landfall in <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> as a minimal Category 1 hurricane. It quickly weakened further to a tropical storm before re-entering the Atlantic to the south of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>. Late on September 15, Hortense transitioned into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> and subsequently merged with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontal_system" title="Frontal system">frontal system</a> about 24 hours later.</p>
<p>The storm produced heavy rainfall and gusty winds as it passed through the Lesser Antilles. On Guadeloupe, damage was most significant to crops, with over 50% of banana plantains destroyed. Localized flooding was reported on <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>. Impact was most significant in Puerto Rico. Across the island, 11,463 houses were severely damaged. About 1.4 million people on Puerto Rico were left without electricity – equivalent to about 40% of the island's population. Damage in Puerto Rico was estimated at $153.4 million (1996 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>). There were also 19 deaths, most of which due to drowning during flash floods. In the Dominican Republic, strong winds damaged a number of buildings and houses, and downed several power lines. Flooding also forced the closure of several roads and impacted 80% of crops in <a href="/wiki/Saman%C3%A1_Province" title="Samaná Province">Samaná Province</a>. At least three fatalities were confirmed in that country. The storm brought strong winds to the Bahamas and <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a>, and lesser winds to Bermuda.</p>
<p>Later in its duration, Hortense struck Nova Scotia, bringing heavy rainfall and hurricane-force winds to isolated locations. There were many power outages, trees blown down, roofs torn away, and roads damaged in the Province. <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a> were also impacted, though not as severely as in Nova Scotia. Damage in Atlantic Canada reached approximately $3.64 million.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hortense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hortense_1996_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="956" data-file-width="1024" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Hortense_1996_track.png/220px-Hortense_1996_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Hortense_1996_track.png/330px-Hortense_1996_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Hortense_1996_track.png/440px-Hortense_1996_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hortense_1996_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> and a broad low-pressure area associated with it exited the west coast of Africa near <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> on August 30. The low-pressure area, which had deepened to 1,005 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.7 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) by August 31, moved south of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>. Although deep convection was minimal on satellite imagery, the system possessed a well-defined low- to mid-level circulation. After crossing several <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> buoys early on September 3, it was determined that the system developed into Tropical Depression Eight at 1200 UTC on that same day, while located about halfway between Cape Verde and the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>. Under the influence of strong high pressure ridge, the depression tracked almost due westward, without significant intensification. Convection remained sporadic and disorganized, with a reconnaissance flight finding a board circulation and a few squall on September 6. While nearing the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a>, upper-level winds reduced to a favorable level, causing strengthening and allowing deep convection to increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Hortense_PR_Landfall.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="587" data-file-width="800" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Hurricane_Hortense_PR_Landfall.gif/220px-Hurricane_Hortense_PR_Landfall.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Hurricane_Hortense_PR_Landfall.gif/330px-Hurricane_Hortense_PR_Landfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Hurricane_Hortense_PR_Landfall.gif/440px-Hurricane_Hortense_PR_Landfall.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Hortense_PR_Landfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hurricane Hortense making landfall in Puerto Rico on September 10</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At 0600 UTC on September 7, the depression had intensified into Tropical Storm Hortense. A reconnaissance flight around that time reported winds of 71 mph (114 km/h), confirming the storm was strengthening. Early on September 8, Hortense struck <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> with winds of 60 mph (95 km/h). Shortly thereafter, intensification slowed, due to increased vertical wind shear by upper-level short wave in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. Convection diminished, with satellite imagery showing a tropical cyclone with the low-level circulation exposed. The short-wave soon left the area, causing wind shear to decrease and convection quickly re-developed.</p>
<p>By early on September 9, Hortense was upgraded to a hurricane, while curving northwestward. Around 0600 UTC on the following day, the storm made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Gu%C3%A1nica,_Puerto_Rico" title="Guánica, Puerto Rico">Guánica, Puerto Rico</a> with winds of 80 mph (130 km/h);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup> this made it the first hurricane landfall on the island since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hugo" title="Hurricane Hugo">Hurricane Hugo</a> in 1989. Initially, most <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">tropical cyclone forecast models</a> anticipated Hortense would pass south of the island and instead strike the Dominican Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-srh_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-srh-2">[2]</a></sup> After two hours, Hortense emerged into the <a href="/wiki/Mona_Passage" title="Mona Passage">Mona Passage</a> and brushed the eastern tip of <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>; <a href="/wiki/Punta_Cana" title="Punta Cana">Punta Cana</a> near the eastern tip of the country reported the calm of the eye on September 10. Land interaction with Hispaniola weakened the storm slightly, though it would soon re-strengthen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>While tracking east of <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a> on September 11, Hortense became a Category 2 hurricane and then to a Category 3 hurricane by early on the following day. Continuing to intensify, Hortense reached Category 4 intensity on September 12. Around that time, a developing trough centered over the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_United_States" title="Eastern United States">Eastern United States</a> caused the storm to curve almost due northward and accelerate. At 0000 UTC on September 13, Hortense attained its peak intensity with a <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained wind</a> speed of 140 mph (220 km/h) and a minimum barometric pressure of 935 mbar (27.6 inHg).</p>
<p>After maintaining Category 4 intensity for 24 hours, the storm weakened back to a Category 3 hurricane. While passing well east of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, Hortense was downgraded to a Category 2 hurricane early on September 14. Later that day, the storm weakened further to a Category 1 hurricane. Early on September 15, Hortense made its final landfall near <a href="/wiki/West_Quoddy,_Nova_Scotia" title="West Quoddy, Nova Scotia">West Quoddy, Nova Scotia</a> with winds of 75 mph (120 km/h). Hortense weakened over Nova Scotia and was a tropical storm when it re-emerged into the Atlantic less than nine hours after landfall. By 1800 UTC on September 15, Hortense transitioned into an extratropical cyclone while just south of Newfoundland. The storm's remnants merged with a frontal system on the following day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hortense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shortly after Hortense intensified into a tropical storm, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm warning</a> was issued by the respective governments from <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">United States Virgin Islands</a>. Late on September 7, about 57 hours before the hurricane's Puerto Rico landfall, a tropical storm warning and hurricane watch was issued for Puerto Rico;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup> however, the watch was discontinued at 1500 UTC on September 8 after the storm experienced increased wind shear in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-srh_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-srh-2">[2]</a></sup> At the same time, a hurricane warning was left after having been issued for the British and United States Virgin Islands 12 hours earlier. At 1500 UTC on September 9 – about fifteen hours before landfall – a hurricane warning was issued in Puerto Rico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup> The downgrade of the warning on the island led residents to underestimate the hurricane's threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-srh_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-srh-2">[2]</a></sup> Before Hortense struck Puerto Rico, the local <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> office warned of the potential of rainfall exceeding 10 in (250 mm), with higher totals in the mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Late on September 9, a hurricane warning was issued for portions of eastern Dominican Republic, which was eventually extended along the country's northern coastline. A tropical storm warning and hurricane watch was issued for the north coast of Haiti to <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B4le-Saint-Nicolas" title="Môle-Saint-Nicolas">St. Nicolas</a>. On September 10, a hurricane warning was issued for the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a> and for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> islands of <a href="/wiki/Inagua" title="Inagua">Inagua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mayaguana" title="Mayaguana">Mayaguana</a>; a hurricane watch was also issued for the Central Bahamas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lesser_Antilles">Lesser Antilles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hortense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lesser Antilles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While still a tropical storm, Hortense moved through the Lesser Antilles with significant amounts of precipitation. A station on Guadeloupe reported a total of 12.5 in (320 mm). Both <a href="/wiki/La_D%C3%A9sirade" title="La Désirade">La Désirade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pointe-%C3%A0-Pitre_International_Airport" title="Pointe-à-Pitre International Airport">Pointe-à-Pitre International Airport</a> observed 81 mph (130 km/h) wind gusts and sustained winds of 53 mph (85 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The storm caused significant crop damage, destroying roughly over 50 percent of the banana plantain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Further south, heavy rains led to scattered flooding in <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which inundated streets, while strong winds knocked down power lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> One man who attempted to drive across a flooded road was rescued from his vehicle seconds before it was swept away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salina98_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-salina98-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Hortense passed south of Saint Croix, the storm produced sustained winds of 39 mph (63 km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Henry_E._Rohlsen_Airport" title="Henry E. Rohlsen Airport">Henry E. Rohlsen Airport</a>, with unofficial gusts to 62 mph (100 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-sd-14">[14]</a></sup> Hortense dropped heavy rainfall that peaked at 18.0 in (460 mm) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christiansted,_United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="Christiansted, United States Virgin Islands">Christiansted</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-rain-15">[15]</a></sup> The rains flooded low-lying areas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederiksted,_United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="Frederiksted, United States Virgin Islands">Frederiksted</a>. On nearby Saint Thomas, the storm dropped 7.0 in (180 mm) of rainfall, with gusts reaching only 30 mph (48 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-sd-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_Antilles">Greater Antilles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hortense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Greater Antilles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Hortense_Flooding.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="295" data-file-width="442" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Hurricane_Hortense_Flooding.jpg/185px-Hurricane_Hortense_Flooding.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Hurricane_Hortense_Flooding.jpg/278px-Hurricane_Hortense_Flooding.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Hurricane_Hortense_Flooding.jpg/370px-Hurricane_Hortense_Flooding.jpg 2x" width="185"/></a>

Flooding caused by Hortense near <a href="/wiki/Guayama,_Puerto_Rico" title="Guayama, Puerto Rico">Guayama, Puerto Rico</a></div>

<p>As Hortense approached Puerto Rico, the eastern half of the circulation had a concentrated area of thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-sd-14">[14]</a></sup> While the hurricane crossed the southwest portion of the island, it dropped heavy rainfall of over 10 in (250 mm) in much of the territory's eastern half. Totals ranged from below 1 in (25 mm) near where the storm moved ashore to a peak of 23.5 in (600 mm) near <a href="/wiki/Cayey,_Puerto_Rico" title="Cayey, Puerto Rico">Cayey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-rain-15">[15]</a></sup> Three stations broke their 12–hour rainfall records and two stations broke their 24–hour rainfall records; the highest 24–hour total was 19.5 in (500 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Las_Piedras,_Puerto_Rico" title="Las Piedras, Puerto Rico">Las Piedras</a>. The rainfall caused widespread flash flooding and increased river <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(hydrology)" title="Discharge (hydrology)">discharge</a>; a station along the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Grande_de_Lo%C3%ADza" title="Río Grande de Loíza">Río Grande de Loíza</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Lorenzo,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Lorenzo, Puerto Rico">San Lorenzo</a> reported a <a href="/wiki/Return_period" title="Return period">1 in 100 year</a> flow rate. Rivers and creeks across the region reached high peaks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-sd-14">[14]</a></sup> and there were several reports of mudslides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup> Sustained winds on the island peaked at 46 mph (74 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Ceiba,_Puerto_Rico" title="Ceiba, Puerto Rico">Ceiba</a>, and gusts reached 62 mph (100 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Carolina,_Puerto_Rico" title="Carolina, Puerto Rico">Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-sd-14">[14]</a></sup> An unofficial wind gust of 109 mph (175 km/h) in the southwest portion of the island may have been caused by the <a href="/wiki/Venturi_effect" title="Venturi effect">Venturi effect</a>, or an acceleration of winds between walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup> Along the southern Puerto Rico coast, Hortense caused coastal flooding and beach erosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-sd-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>An overflown river near San Juan flooded several homes. Across the island, Hortense left about 1.3 million people without power and 1.1 million customers without water. During the storm, 10,563 people evacuated to storm shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-sd-14">[14]</a></sup> Across the island, 11,463 houses were severely damaged by Hortense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup> The hurricane left $128.4 million in crop damage, primarily to coffee, plantains, and bananas. There was another $25 million from road damage. There were 19 deaths on the island, many of whom due to flash flood drownings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-sd-14">[14]</a></sup> Some 1,400,000 people, about 40% of the population, lost power during and after the storm, straining the cleanup effort in the following days and weeks with lack of air conditioning and spoiled food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-augusta_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-augusta-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

Flooding in Puerto Rico</div>

<p>While Hortense was near the eastern tip of the Dominican Republic, Punta Cana reported sustained winds of 92 mph (148 km/h), which was the highest land wind report associated with the hurricane. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerto_Plata_(city)" title="Puerto Plata (city)">Puerto Plata</a> along the northern coast also reported hurricane-force winds. High winds destroyed a school and church, damaged several houses, and downed several power lines. Like its effects in Puerto Rico, Hortense dropped heavy rainfall in the Dominican Republic, peaking at 19.25 in (489 mm) in <a href="/wiki/San_Rafael_del_Yuma" title="San Rafael del Yuma">San Rafael del Yuma</a>. While passing the region, the hurricane produced a 9 ft (2.7 m) <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>. The combination of the surge and heavy rainfall produced flooding that closed roadways. Hortense damaged 80% of the crops in <a href="/wiki/Saman%C3%A1_Province" title="Samaná Province">Samaná</a>. In the country, the hurricane killed three people and left 21 missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere">Elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hortense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Grand_Turk_Island" title="Grand Turk Island">Grand Turk Island</a>, wind gusts reached 90 mph (140 km/h) while Hortense passed nearby; the same station reported sustained winds of 60 mph (97 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hort_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Hort-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, threatened by their second major hurricane in two weeks, fared well from the storm, with little damage and no casualties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-augusta_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-augusta-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, a sustained wind speed of 34 mph (55 km/h) and a gust to 48 mph (77 km/h) were measured on September 13; slightly lesser winds were reported the following day. The storm also brought rainfall to the island, though total amounts are unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, Hortense dropped heavy rainfall and caused strong winds, causing power outages, uprooted trees, and moderate structural damage amounting to $3 million (1996 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHC96_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-CHC96-19">[19]</a></sup> Rainfall was light on <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a>, with the highest observed being 1.7 inches (43 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Charlottetown" title="Charlottetown">Charlottetown</a>. Strong winds were reported throughout the province. Gusts reached 52 mph (84 km/h) in Charlottetown and 80 mph (130 km/h) elsewhere. The winds downed power lines, leaving numerous residents without electricity, especially in Charlottetown, <a href="/wiki/East_Point,_Prince_Edward_Island" title="East Point, Prince Edward Island">East Point</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montague,_Prince_Edward_Island" title="Montague, Prince Edward Island">Montague</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murray_River,_Prince_Edward_Island" title="Murray River, Prince Edward Island">Murray River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Souris,_Prince_Edward_Island" title="Souris, Prince Edward Island">Souris</a>. Effects were almost nil in <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, limited to rainfall peaking at 2.1 inches (53 mm), though most of it was a storm system that preceded Hortense's passage. Sustained winds in the province were light, reaching about 27 mph (43 km/h).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hortense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>On September 11, President Bill Clinton declared a major disaster in 68 of the 78 <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Municipalities of Puerto Rico">Municipalities of Puerto Rico</a>; this allowed federal funding for debris removal, repairing public roads and buildings, and assistance to individually affected residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name Hortense was retired in Spring of 1997 following this <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">storm</a>, and will not be used again in this basin. It was replaced with Hanna in the <a href="/wiki/2002_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2002 Atlantic hurricane season">2002 Atlantic hurricane season</a>.</p>
